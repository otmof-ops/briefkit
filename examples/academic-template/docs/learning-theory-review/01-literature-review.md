# Literature Review: Learning Theory Traditions

## Introduction

The study of how humans learn has generated a succession of theoretical paradigms, each responding to the limitations of its predecessors while introducing new explanatory frameworks and design prescriptions. This chapter surveys four major traditions --- behaviorism, cognitivism, constructivism, and connectivism --- tracing their core commitments, key contributors, and implications for instructional design. The purpose is not to declare a winner but to map the intellectual landscape that contemporary designers must navigate.

## Behaviorism: Learning as Observable Behavior Change

The behaviorist tradition, dominant from the 1910s through the 1960s, defined learning as a relatively permanent change in observable behavior resulting from environmental contingencies. Edward Thorndike's law of effect (1911) established the foundational principle: responses followed by satisfying consequences are strengthened, while those followed by annoying consequences are weakened. Ivan Pavlov's classical conditioning research demonstrated that organisms learn to associate stimuli, producing conditioned responses that mirror unconditioned ones.

B.F. Skinner extended these principles through his work on operant conditioning, demonstrating that the consequences of behavior --- reinforcement and punishment, delivered on various schedules --- systematically shape behavioral repertoires. Skinner's (1958) teaching machines and programmed instruction represented the first systematic application of learning theory to instructional design: content was broken into small frames, each requiring an active response and providing immediate feedback, with progression contingent on demonstrated mastery.

Robert Gagne bridged behaviorism and the emerging cognitive paradigm through his "Conditions of Learning" (1965), which proposed a taxonomy of five learning outcome types --- verbal information, intellectual skills, cognitive strategies, motor skills, and attitudes --- each requiring different instructional conditions. His nine events of instruction (gaining attention, informing learners of objectives, stimulating recall of prior learning, presenting the stimulus, providing learning guidance, eliciting performance, providing feedback, assessing performance, and enhancing retention and transfer) remain the most widely taught instructional design heuristic in graduate programs worldwide. Robert Mager's (1962) behavioral objectives framework, requiring specification of observable behavior, conditions of performance, and performance criteria, imposed a disciplined precision on curriculum development that persists in standards-based education systems.

The behaviorist legacy in instructional design includes the emphasis on measurable objectives, systematic sequencing, active responding, immediate feedback, and mastery-based progression. These principles remain embedded in computer-based training, competency-based education, and adaptive learning systems, even when the underlying theoretical rationale has shifted to cognitive or constructivist frameworks.

## Cognitivism: Learning as Information Processing

The cognitive revolution of the 1960s, catalyzed by Noam Chomsky's critique of Skinner's verbal behavior, George Miller's research on working memory capacity, and Ulric Neisser's foundational text "Cognitive Psychology" (1967), redirected attention from observable behavior to the internal mental processes mediating between stimulus and response. The mind was reconceived as an information processor, analogous to a computer, that encodes, stores, transforms, and retrieves representations of the external world.

The Atkinson-Shiffrin multi-store memory model (1968) proposed three memory systems --- sensory register, short-term (working) memory, and long-term memory --- with distinct capacities and durations. Craik and Lockhart's (1972) levels-of-processing framework challenged the structural model, arguing that memory durability depends on the depth of processing at encoding rather than the structural store in which information resides. Both frameworks generated design prescriptions: the multi-store model emphasized rehearsal, chunking, and elaboration to transfer information from working to long-term memory; the levels-of-processing framework emphasized meaningful engagement over rote repetition.

David Ausubel's (1968) theory of meaningful learning argued that the single most important factor influencing learning is what the learner already knows. His concept of advance organizers --- introductory material presented before new content to activate and structure prior knowledge --- provided a practical strategy for bridging existing and new knowledge. Ausubel's distinction between meaningful and rote learning mapped directly onto design decisions about whether to present material in ways that connect to existing cognitive structures or merely require memorization.

John Sweller's cognitive load theory (1988) has become the most influential cognitive framework for instructional design. Building on the established limitations of working memory (approximately four to seven elements, decaying within approximately 20 seconds without rehearsal), Sweller distinguished three types of cognitive load: intrinsic load (reflecting the inherent complexity of the material), extraneous load (imposed by poor instructional design), and germane load (devoted to productive schema construction). The theory generated a suite of empirically validated instructional effects: the worked example effect, the split attention effect, the redundancy effect, the modality effect, and the expertise reversal effect. Each provides specific, actionable design guidance grounded in the architecture of human cognition.

David Merrill's (2002) First Principles of Instruction synthesized across cognitive theories to identify five principles with convergent empirical support: learning is promoted when instruction is problem-centered, activates existing knowledge, demonstrates the skill to be learned, requires learner application, and integrates new knowledge into the learner's world. Meta-analytic evidence (Frick, Chadha, Watson, and Zlatkovska, 2009) confirmed that instruction implementing more of these principles produces proportionally greater learning gains.

## Constructivism: Learning as Knowledge Construction

Constructivist theories, drawing on Jean Piaget's developmental epistemology, Lev Vygotsky's sociocultural psychology, and John Dewey's pragmatist philosophy, hold that learners actively construct knowledge through interaction with their environment rather than passively receiving transmitted information. The constructivist thesis is epistemological: knowledge is not a copy of external reality stored in the mind but a construction built by the learner through processes of assimilation and accommodation.

Piaget's developmental theory proposed that children construct increasingly sophisticated cognitive structures (schemas) through interaction with the physical and social environment. Cognitive conflict --- the experience of encountering information that contradicts existing schemas --- drives development through the complementary processes of assimilation (incorporating new information into existing schemas) and accommodation (restructuring schemas to account for new information). For instructional design, Piaget's work implied that instruction must be developmentally appropriate, that learners need direct experience with materials and concepts, and that cognitive conflict is a productive catalyst for learning rather than an obstacle to be avoided.

Vygotsky's social constructivism emphasized that knowledge is co-constructed through social interaction and mediated by cultural tools, particularly language. His zone of proximal development (ZPD) --- the gap between what a learner can accomplish independently and what they can accomplish with expert guidance --- provided a powerful framework for calibrating instructional support. The scaffolding metaphor, introduced by Wood, Bruner, and Ross (1976), operationalized the ZPD concept: instructors provide temporary, structured support within the learner's ZPD and gradually withdraw it as the learner develops independent competence.

Lave and Wenger's (1991) situated learning theory and the concept of communities of practice extended constructivism by arguing that learning is inseparable from the social and physical context in which it occurs. Their work challenged the assumption that knowledge can be decontextualized and transferred across settings without loss, implying that effective instruction must embed learning in authentic contexts that mirror real-world practice. Brown, Collins, and Duguid's (1989) cognitive apprenticeship model translated these principles into design strategies: modeling, coaching, scaffolding, fading, articulation, reflection, and exploration.

## Connectivism: Learning in Digital Networks

George Siemens' (2005) connectivism proposed a theory of learning for the digital age, arguing that traditional theories developed in an era when knowledge was relatively stable and individually held fail to account for learning in an environment of information abundance, rapid knowledge obsolescence, and distributed expertise. Connectivism holds that learning resides in networks of people, databases, and digital tools; that the capacity to know is more critical than current knowledge; and that nurturing and maintaining connections is a primary learning activity.

Siemens articulated eight principles of connectivism: learning and knowledge rest in diversity of opinions; learning is a process of connecting specialized nodes or information sources; learning may reside in non-human appliances; the capacity to know more is more critical than what is currently known; nurturing and maintaining connections is needed to facilitate continual learning; the ability to see connections between fields, ideas, and concepts is a core skill; currency (accurate, up-to-date knowledge) is the intent of all connectivist learning activities; and decision-making is itself a learning process.

While connectivism's status as a formal learning theory remains debated (Verhagen, 2006, argued it is a pedagogical view rather than a theory; Kop and Hill, 2008, questioned its empirical base), it has exerted significant influence on the design of massive open online courses (MOOCs), personal learning environments, and network-based professional development. The mixed results of early MOOCs --- high enrollment but low completion rates averaging 5-15% --- have tempered initial enthusiasm while stimulating research into the conditions under which network-based learning succeeds.

## Comparative Analysis

The following table summarizes the four paradigms across key dimensions:

| Dimension | Behaviorism | Cognitivism | Constructivism | Connectivism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nature of knowledge** | Objective reality | Mental representations | Personal/social construction | Distributed across networks |
| **Role of learner** | Passive recipient | Active processor | Active constructor | Network navigator |
| **Role of instructor** | Environmental arranger | Information architect | Facilitator/guide | Curator/connector |
| **Primary mechanism** | Reinforcement | Schema construction | Assimilation/accommodation | Connection formation |
| **Key contributors** | Thorndike, Skinner, Gagne | Ausubel, Sweller, Merrill | Piaget, Vygotsky, Lave | Siemens, Downes |
| **Design emphasis** | Objectives, sequencing, feedback | Cognitive load, worked examples | Authentic tasks, scaffolding | Networks, learner pathways |
| **Assessment focus** | Observable behavior | Knowledge structures | Performance in context | Portfolio of connections |
| **Primary limitation** | Ignores internal processes | Underestimates social context | Implementation difficulty | Weak empirical base |

## Conclusion

Each paradigm illuminates aspects of learning that others obscure. Behaviorism provides the discipline of measurable objectives and systematic feedback. Cognitivism reveals the processing constraints that determine what learners can absorb. Constructivism foregrounds the active, social, and contextual nature of knowledge building. Connectivism addresses learning in information-rich, networked environments. The challenge for instructional designers is not to choose a single paradigm but to draw on the strengths of each in principled, evidence-informed ways --- a challenge addressed in the theoretical framework that follows.
