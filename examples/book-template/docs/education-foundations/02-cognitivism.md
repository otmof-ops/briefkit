# Chapter 2: Cognitivism

## Introduction

The cognitive revolution of the 1960s transformed the study of learning by opening the "black box" that behaviorism had declared off-limits. Where behaviorists studied the relationship between environmental stimuli and behavioral responses, cognitivists investigated the internal mental processes --- attention, perception, encoding, storage, retrieval, and metacognition --- that mediate between input and output. This chapter traces the development of cognitive learning theory from the information processing model through schema theory, cognitive load theory, and the taxonomies of learning that continue to guide instructional design.

## The Information Processing Model

The foundational metaphor of cognitive psychology is the computer: the mind, like a computer, receives information (input), processes it through a series of transformations, stores it in memory, and produces responses (output). While this metaphor has been criticized as overly mechanistic, it provided a productive framework for investigating the structures and processes of human cognition.

The Atkinson-Shiffrin multi-store model (1968) proposed three memory systems with distinct characteristics. The sensory register holds incoming sensory information for a very brief duration --- approximately 250 milliseconds for visual information (iconic memory) and 3-4 seconds for auditory information (echoic memory). Attention acts as a filter, selecting a small portion of sensory information for transfer to the next system. Short-term memory (later reconceptualized as working memory by Baddeley and Hitch, 1974) holds attended information for approximately 20 seconds, with a capacity of approximately four to seven chunks. Rehearsal and elaborative encoding can transfer information from working memory to long-term memory, which has effectively unlimited capacity and duration.

George Miller's (1956) landmark paper, "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two," established that working memory capacity is limited to approximately seven chunks of information. The concept of chunking --- combining individual elements into meaningful units based on prior knowledge --- explained how experts can process far more information than novices despite having the same working memory capacity. A chess grandmaster perceives board positions not as individual pieces but as meaningful configurations (chunks) that encode both structural and strategic information.

Craik and Lockhart's (1972) levels-of-processing framework offered an alternative to the structural model, arguing that memory durability depends on the depth of processing at encoding. Shallow processing (attending to surface features like the font or sound of a word) produces fragile memory traces, while deep processing (attending to meaning, making connections to prior knowledge, generating examples) produces durable ones. This framework has direct implications for instructional design: activities that require deep, meaningful processing of content produce better learning outcomes than activities that require only surface-level engagement.

## Schema Theory

The concept of schemas --- organized knowledge structures stored in long-term memory --- provides the bridge between information processing and learning. Schemas are not passive storage containers but active frameworks that guide attention, interpretation, encoding, and retrieval. When a reader encounters the sentence "The doctor examined the patient," their physician schema activates a network of related knowledge (examination rooms, stethoscopes, diagnoses, medical training) that supports comprehension and inference.

Schema theory explains several important learning phenomena. Experts process information faster and more accurately than novices because they possess rich, well-organized schemas that enable automatic pattern recognition and efficient chunking. Prior knowledge facilitates new learning because existing schemas provide anchoring points for integrating new information. Transfer of learning occurs when schemas are sufficiently abstract and flexible to apply across contexts. Misconceptions are resistant to correction because they are embedded in coherent (if inaccurate) schema structures that assimilate contradictory evidence rather than accommodating to it.

For instructional design, schema theory prescribes that instruction should activate relevant prior knowledge before introducing new content, organize new information in ways that connect to existing schemas, provide multiple examples and contexts to promote abstract and flexible schema construction, and address misconceptions directly through cognitive conflict strategies that force schema restructuring.

## Ausubel's Meaningful Learning

David Ausubel's (1968) theory of meaningful learning begins with the proposition that the most important factor influencing learning is what the learner already knows. Meaningful learning occurs when new information is deliberately connected to existing knowledge structures; rote learning occurs when new information is memorized without such connections.

Ausubel's concept of advance organizers --- introductory material presented before new content at a higher level of abstraction, generality, and inclusiveness --- provides a practical strategy for bridging existing and new knowledge. Expository organizers provide a conceptual framework for entirely new material; comparative organizers highlight similarities and differences between new material and familiar concepts. Meta-analytic evidence (Luiten, Ames, and Ackerson, 1980) supports the effectiveness of advance organizers, with stronger effects for lower-ability learners and unfamiliar material.

Ausubel's theory also introduced the distinction between reception learning (the learner receives the material in its final form) and discovery learning (the learner must discover the content independently before it can be meaningfully incorporated). Importantly, Ausubel argued that both reception and discovery learning can be either meaningful or rote. Meaningful reception learning --- in which well-organized information is presented and deliberately connected to prior knowledge --- can be more effective than rote discovery learning in which students manipulate materials without achieving understanding.

## Cognitive Load Theory

John Sweller's cognitive load theory (CLT), developed from the late 1980s onward, has become the most influential cognitive framework for instructional design. CLT is built on two well-established premises: working memory is severely limited in capacity when processing novel information, and long-term memory is effectively unlimited and serves as the primary repository of learned knowledge in the form of schemas.

CLT distinguishes three types of cognitive load that compete for working memory resources:

**Intrinsic load** reflects the inherent complexity of the material being learned --- specifically, the number of interacting elements that must be processed simultaneously. The sentence "Mammals are warm-blooded" has low element interactivity (each concept can be understood independently). Solving a multi-step algebra equation has high element interactivity (each step depends on the preceding steps and the learner must hold multiple elements in working memory simultaneously). Intrinsic load is determined by the nature of the material and the learner's prior knowledge; as schemas develop, previously interacting elements become chunked into single elements, effectively reducing intrinsic load.

**Extraneous load** reflects the demands imposed by the instructional format rather than the content itself. A diagram with its explanatory text on a separate page creates extraneous load by requiring the learner to split attention between two information sources and mentally integrate them. The same diagram with integrated labels eliminates this extraneous load. Poor instructional design increases extraneous load; good design minimizes it.

**Germane load** reflects the cognitive effort devoted to schema construction and automation --- the productive processing that directly contributes to learning. Activities that promote deep processing, self-explanation, comparison, and elaboration increase germane load.

The following table compares the original and revised Bloom's taxonomy, which provides a complementary framework for classifying the cognitive demands of learning tasks:

| Original Taxonomy (1956) | Revised Taxonomy (2001) | Cognitive Process | Example Verb |
|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge | Remember | Retrieve relevant knowledge from long-term memory | Define, list, recall, identify |
| Comprehension | Understand | Construct meaning from instructional messages | Explain, summarize, paraphrase, classify |
| Application | Apply | Carry out or use a procedure in a given situation | Execute, implement, solve, demonstrate |
| Analysis | Analyze | Break material into constituent parts and determine relationships | Differentiate, organize, compare, deconstruct |
| Synthesis | Evaluate | Make judgments based on criteria and standards | Critique, judge, justify, assess |
| Evaluation | Create | Put elements together to form a coherent whole or original product | Design, construct, produce, generate |

The revised taxonomy (Anderson and Krathwohl, 2001) added a knowledge dimension (factual, conceptual, procedural, metacognitive) that intersects with the cognitive process dimension to create a two-dimensional classification framework. This revision reflects the cognitive understanding that the type of knowledge being processed interacts with the cognitive operation being performed to determine the overall complexity and cognitive demand of a learning task.

## Empirically Validated Instructional Effects

Cognitive load theory has generated a substantial body of empirically validated instructional effects, each providing specific design guidance:

The **worked example effect**: novice learners learn more effectively from studying worked examples than from solving equivalent problems, because worked examples reduce the extraneous load associated with means-ends problem-solving search.

The **split attention effect**: integrating multiple sources of information (text and diagrams, for example) into a single unified format reduces extraneous load compared with presenting them separately.

The **redundancy effect**: presenting the same information in multiple forms (narrating text that is also displayed on screen, for instance) increases extraneous load rather than reinforcing learning, because the learner must process redundant information and reconcile the two sources.

The **modality effect**: presenting some information visually and some auditorily exploits both the visual and auditory channels of working memory, increasing effective capacity compared with presenting all information in a single modality.

The **expertise reversal effect**: instructional techniques that benefit novices (such as worked examples and integrated formats) can impair learning for advanced learners who already possess relevant schemas. This effect has profound implications for adaptive instructional design, as it means that optimal instruction must change as the learner develops expertise.

## Implications for Instructional Design

The cognitive tradition provides instructional designers with an evidence-based understanding of how the human cognitive architecture constrains and enables learning. Design decisions about content sequencing, multimedia presentation, practice activities, and assessment can all be informed by cognitive principles. The key practical takeaways are: minimize extraneous load through clear, well-organized presentation; manage intrinsic load through sequencing from simple to complex; promote germane load through activities that require deep processing; activate prior knowledge to support schema-based encoding; and adapt instructional support to the learner's developing expertise.

## Summary

Cognitivism transformed the study of learning by investigating the internal mental processes that behaviorism excluded. The information processing model, schema theory, meaningful learning theory, Bloom's taxonomy, and cognitive load theory together provide a comprehensive and empirically grounded framework for understanding how humans acquire, store, and retrieve knowledge. These frameworks generate specific, actionable design prescriptions that have been validated across domains and learner populations, making the cognitive tradition the most directly applicable to instructional design practice.
