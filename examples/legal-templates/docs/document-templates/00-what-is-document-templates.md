# What Is Document Templates?

**Section:** AF-PRAC-03
**Document Type:** Orientation
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

---

## What Is This?

Document templates are master legal documents — contracts, court forms, corporate records, property instruments, estate planning documents, regulatory filings, and correspondence — pre-drafted with bracketed variables, clause-by-clause annotations, jurisdiction-specific variation notes, and guidance on common pitfalls.

A template is not a finished document. It is a professionally constructed starting point that encodes the knowledge of what a particular document type needs to contain, what each clause does, where the risks lie, and how the requirements differ across jurisdictions. When a lawyer or support staff member works from a well-maintained template rather than drafting from scratch, they get a faster first draft, fewer omissions, built-in compliance notes, and a structured prompt for the judgment calls that still need to be made for the specific transaction or matter.

This section covers seven categories of template:

- **Contract templates** — non-disclosure agreements, services agreements, employment contracts, and residential leases, with full annotated text and jurisdiction variations across Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand.
- **Litigation templates** — statements of claim, defenses, subpoenas, notices of motion, and affidavits of service, with court-specific form numbers and filing requirements.
- **Corporate templates** — company formation documents, shareholder agreements, board resolutions, and constitutional documents.
- **Property templates** — contracts for the sale of land, transfer instruments, mortgage documentation, and leasing agreements.
- **Estate planning templates** — wills, powers of attorney, and advance care directives.
- **Regulatory compliance templates** — privacy impact assessments, data breach response plans, compliance policies, and regulatory submissions.
- **Correspondence templates** — letters of demand, without-prejudice letters, Calderbank offers, and engagement letters.

## Why Does It Matter?

Templates matter because legal documents are how legal rights are created, exercised, and protected. A contract that does not address what happens on insolvency leaves a party exposed when the other side collapses. A will that does not include a substitution clause may fail to distribute the estate as intended if a named beneficiary predeceases the testator. A subpoena that is not specific enough will be set aside; one that is too broad will be challenged as oppressive.

The risks of drafting from scratch every time are real: omission of essential clauses, inadvertent use of outdated law, failure to adapt for jurisdictional requirements, and inconsistency between documents in the same transaction. Templates address all of these risks by building institutional knowledge into the document itself.

For practitioners in smaller firms or those working in areas outside their usual specialty, templates are a risk management tool as much as an efficiency tool. The annotations explain not just what each clause says but why it exists — which means that when circumstances call for departing from the standard form, the practitioner understands what they are departing from and why.

Templates also underpin modern practice management. Document automation systems — which use conditional logic and variable data to generate completed documents from templates — depend on well-maintained, well-annotated templates as their foundation. A firm that has invested in quality templates is positioned to deploy automation; one that has not is not.

## Who Uses This?

**Solicitors and transactional lawyers** use contract, corporate, and property templates constantly. A significant proportion of commercial and private client work involves producing standard-form documents that follow a known pattern, adapted for the specific transaction.

**Litigators** rely on litigation templates for the procedural documents that frame every dispute: pleadings, applications, affidavits of service, and correspondence with the opposing party. Court forms in particular must comply with prescribed form numbers and formatting requirements.

**Estate planning practitioners** work from will and power of attorney templates that must be adapted for the testator's specific wishes and the jurisdiction's formal requirements.

**In-house lawyers** in corporate legal departments use templates to standardize the commercial documents their organizations produce at volume — NDAs, supplier agreements, employment contracts — and to reduce dependence on external legal advice for routine transactions.

**Law students** studying transactional practice or drafting subjects will find the annotated templates useful for understanding how the abstract legal principles taught in contracts, property, and company law courses translate into actual document language.

**Paralegals and legal support staff** use templates to prepare first drafts under lawyer supervision, understanding which fields to complete, which options to select, and which matters require instruction before proceeding.
