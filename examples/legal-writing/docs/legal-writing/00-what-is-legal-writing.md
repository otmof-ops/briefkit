# What Is Legal Writing?

**Section:** AF-PRAC-02
**Document Type:** Orientation
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

---

## What Is This?

Legal writing is the practice of communicating legal information and analysis in written form. It covers every document a lawyer produces: internal research memos, letters of advice, court submissions, affidavits, pleadings, contracts, and legislation. Each of these has a different audience, a different purpose, and a different set of conventions — but all of them rest on the same foundational principles.

The section begins with those principles: clarity, precision, plain language, active voice, audience awareness, sentence structure, and gender-neutral drafting. These are not stylistic preferences. They are functional requirements. A submission that a judge cannot follow in the time available will not be persuasive. An advice letter that a client cannot understand will not help them make a decision. A contract clause that is ambiguous will be disputed and may be construed against the party that drafted it.

From the principles, the section moves into the major document types. The legal memorandum is the workhorse of internal legal practice — an objective, structured analysis of a legal question written for a supervising lawyer or team. Letters of advice translate the findings of a memorandum into language suitable for clients. Court submissions argue a party's case to a judge. Affidavits and declarations present factual evidence on oath. Pleadings define the issues in litigation. And legislative drafting — the most technically demanding form of legal writing — creates the statutory text that becomes binding law.

---

## Why Does It Matter?

Writing is how law is done. Research without writing is just reading. Judgment without writing is just opinion. The client's interests, the court's decision, and the legislature's policy are all carried into effect through written text.

The quality of legal writing has direct practical consequences. A poorly structured court submission fails to persuade the court, even if the underlying legal position is strong. A vaguely drafted contract term creates a dispute where none should exist. A legal advice letter that hedges every proposition with qualifications leaves the client unable to decide what to do. Conversely, a well-crafted submission that front-loads its key points, supports them with synthesized authority, and addresses the counter-arguments can win a case that looked difficult.

Legal writing standards are also matters of professional obligation. Courts in all five covered jurisdictions have practice directions and style rules that legal documents must comply with. Failure to follow them can result in documents being rejected, costs orders, or worse. The plain language movement — now embedded in legislation across Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand — has shifted the expectation: complex, archaic legal language is no longer a sign of expertise. It is increasingly treated as a failure of communication.

For junior lawyers, the ability to write clearly and precisely is the most visible measure of competence. A supervisor reading a research memorandum is assessing not just whether the writer found the right cases, but whether they can explain what the cases mean and why they matter to the specific question.

## Who Uses This?

**Every admitted lawyer** writes in the course of practice. Transactional lawyers draft contracts, disclosure documents, and regulatory filings. Litigators prepare pleadings, submissions, affidavits, and interlocutory applications. Advisory lawyers produce memoranda and advice letters. All of them need the foundational principles covered in this section.

**Law students** encounter legal writing from their first year: case notes, legal problem essays, research memoranda, and moots. The conventions in this section — particularly the IRAC/CREAC methodology for structuring legal analysis — are directly applicable to law school assessment.

**Legislative drafters** in government offices use specialized techniques covered in the legislative drafting document. This is a distinct subspecialty within legal writing, requiring the most precise control of language of any form of legal work.

**Paralegals and legal support staff** who assist with document production benefit from understanding the principles underlying the documents they work on, particularly the rules about defined terms, active voice, and document structure.

**Clients** are the ultimate audience for a significant portion of legal writing. Understanding what clear, client-appropriate writing looks like helps lawyers move from the default of writing for their peers to writing for the person who actually needs to understand and act on the advice.
