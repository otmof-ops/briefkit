# Principles of Legal Writing

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-01
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf`](columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf), [`precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf`](precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf), [`persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf`](persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf)

Every form of legal writing --- memoranda, court submissions, advice letters, pleadings, contracts, and statutes --- rests on a common set of principles. These principles govern clarity, precision, structure, and audience awareness. A lawyer who understands and applies these foundational principles can write effectively across all document types. A lawyer who lacks them produces writing that is obscure, imprecise, and ineffective, regardless of the quality of the underlying legal analysis.

This document establishes the foundational principles that apply across all forms of legal writing addressed in this section. Subsequent documents apply these principles to specific document types.

---

## Clarity

Clarity is the first obligation of legal writing. A document that cannot be understood cannot serve its purpose, regardless of the sophistication of its analysis. This seems obvious, but the history of legal writing has been marked by a persistent tendency toward obscurity.

### The Plain Language Standard

The plain language movement holds that legal documents should be written in language that the intended audience can understand on first reading. This does not mean oversimplification or sacrificing legal precision. It means eliminating unnecessary complexity --- the complexity that serves no function other than to make the writer seem learned or the document seem more formal.

**Brief history of the plain language movement in law:**

- **1970s**: Consumer protection legislation in the US and UK began requiring "plain English" in consumer contracts. New York enacted the first US plain language law in 1978, requiring consumer contracts to be "clear and coherent."
- **1980s**: The Australian Law Reform Commission published reports advocating plain language in legislation. Victoria's *Legal Profession Practice Act 1986* included plain language requirements. The Australian Tax Office began its landmark rewrite of the Income Tax Assessment Act.
- **1990s**: The UK's Office of Parliamentary Counsel adopted plain language guidelines. New Zealand's Legislation Advisory Committee published drafting guidelines favoring plain language. Canada's Department of Justice published bilingual plain language standards.
- **2000s**: The EU began publishing guidelines for "clear writing" in EU legislation and regulatory documents. Plain language requirements spread through Australian state and territory legislation.
- **2010**: The US enacted the *Plain Writing Act of 2010* (Pub. L. No. 111-274), requiring federal agencies to use plain language in public communications.
- **2020s**: AI-assisted drafting tools are increasingly incorporating plain language scoring. Multiple courts have issued guidance praising clear, concise submissions and criticizing unnecessarily complex ones.

The evidence base for plain language is now substantial: plain-language legal documents are read faster, understood better, complied with more readily, and less likely to be disputed. The case for plain language is not just aesthetic; it is empirical.

### Core Plain Language Principles

**1. Use short sentences**: Aim for an average sentence length of 20--25 words. No sentence should exceed 40 words unless the structure is very clear and the complexity is necessary. Long sentences --- particularly those with multiple subordinate clauses, qualifications, and parenthetical exceptions --- force the reader to hold multiple ideas in suspension simultaneously, which imposes cognitive load and increases reading errors.

**2. Use active voice as the default**: "The director must file the report" is clearer than "The report must be filed by the director." Active voice identifies who is responsible for the action; passive voice often obscures it. See the full treatment of active versus passive voice below.

**3. Use concrete language**: "The company must pay the debt within 14 days" rather than "The outstanding obligation shall be satisfied within such period as may be prescribed." Concrete language is testable: you can tell when 14 days have passed. Vague language creates disputes.

**4. Use common words**: "Begin" rather than "commence." "Use" rather than "utilize." "End" rather than "terminate." "About" rather than "approximately" unless the difference between "about" and "approximately" matters. Common words are processed faster; they carry no risk of the reader assigning a different meaning to the technical word.

**5. Eliminate redundant pairs**: Legal writing is infested with redundant pairs that exist through tradition, not function:
- "Null and void" → "void"
- "Each and every" → "each" or "every"
- "Cease and desist" → "stop"
- "Terms and conditions" → "terms" (unless they are genuinely distinct)
- "By and between" → "between"
- "Agrees and covenants" → "agrees"
- "Give, devise and bequeath" → "give" (or "leave")

**6. One idea per sentence**: If a sentence makes two separate points, break it into two sentences. If a condition is attached to a rule, consider whether to state the rule first and the condition second: "An employee is entitled to four weeks' annual leave. The entitlement accrues at the rate of one week for each three months of employment." Not: "An employee is entitled to four weeks' annual leave, provided that such entitlement shall accrue at the rate of one week for each three months of continuous employment completed by the said employee."

**7. Front-load important information**: Put the subject and verb near the beginning of the sentence. The reader should encounter the main action early, not after wading through introductory qualifications.

**Example rewrite**:

*Before*: "Notwithstanding the provisions of the preceding paragraph and subject to any contrary agreement made in writing between the parties and executed by all parties to this Agreement, the employee shall be under an obligation to provide not less than four weeks' notice in writing of his or her intention to terminate the employment."

*After*: "The employee must give at least four weeks' written notice to terminate employment. This requirement may be varied by a separate written agreement signed by all parties."

Same legal effect. Forty-six words reduced to twenty-six. Three qualifications restructured as a note, not a proviso. Readable on first reading.

### When Complexity is Necessary

Plain language does not mean imprecision. Some legal propositions require technical terminology or carefully structured sentences to achieve their function. The test is: does this complexity serve the communication, or does it merely add formality?

**Acceptable complexity**: "The duty of care owed by a manufacturer to the ultimate consumer of its product is established by *Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562 and does not depend on a contractual relationship between them." (Technical terms are necessary and defined by law; the sentence structure is justified by the proposition's complexity.)

**Unacceptable complexity**: "Notwithstanding the provisions heretofore set forth in the foregoing paragraphs, and subject to the conditions precedent hereinafter specified, the party of the first part shall be under an obligation to..." (Every element of complexity can be eliminated without loss of meaning.)

## Precision

Clarity without precision is useful but legally insufficient. A legal document must not only be understood but understood in exactly the way the writer intends. Imprecision creates disputes; in a contract, legislation, or pleading, imprecision may determine the outcome of litigation.

### Defined Terms

Defined terms create precision by fixing the meaning of words within a document.

**Rules for defined terms**:

1. **Define terms that bear a specific meaning within the document**: Particularly where the term has an ordinary meaning that differs from or is narrower than the intended meaning. "Agreement" might be defined to include all amendments, attachments, and schedules. "Business Day" might be defined to exclude public holidays in a specified jurisdiction.

2. **Use the defined term consistently**: Once defined, always use the term in its defined sense. Never use the defined word in its ordinary sense alongside the defined sense. If the definition of "Services" covers specified IT services, do not later refer to "services" in the ordinary sense without making it clear you are using the un-defined word.

3. **Signal defined terms**: In contracts and legislation, defined terms are typically capitalized or marked on first use with quotation marks: "the Agreement." In legal writing generally, the convention should be consistent throughout the document.

4. **Place definitions appropriately**: In longer documents (contracts, legislation), group all definitions in a definitions section at the beginning. For terms used only in a single provision, define them in that provision: "In this section, 'control' means..."

5. **Do not over-define**: Common words with clear ordinary meanings do not need definitions unless the ordinary meaning is genuinely unsuitable for the document's purpose. Defining "day" to mean "a period of 24 hours" in an ordinary context is unnecessary and adds bulk without clarity.

6. **"Means" vs "includes"**: The distinction between these two formulations is critical in both contracts and legislation:
   - "Means": The term is exhaustively defined. It has only the meanings listed.
   - "Includes": The term retains its ordinary meaning, expanded to include the listed items.
   - "Means and includes": Hybrid; both the listed items and other items within the ordinary meaning.

### Ambiguity

Ambiguity in legal writing is not a minor stylistic flaw. It is a functional defect that may lead to litigation, produce unintended legal consequences, or make a document unenforceable.

**Types of ambiguity:**

| Type | Description | Example | Solution |
|------|-------------|---------|----------|
| Lexical | A word bears multiple meanings | "Bank" (financial institution or river bank); "discharge" (fulfill or terminate) | Context or definition |
| Syntactic | Sentence structure permits two readings | "Visiting relatives can be boring" (they bore you, or you bore them?) | Restructure the sentence |
| Referential | A pronoun or reference is unclear | "The director told the shareholder she was in breach" (who is she?) | Use the noun instead of the pronoun |
| Scope | The scope of a modifier is unclear | "Red cars and trucks" (all trucks, or only red trucks?) | Insert "both red" or restructure |
| Sequential | The order of conditions is unclear | "If X and Y, then Z" (must both X and Y occur, or either?) | Use "both" or "either" explicitly |

**Eliminating ambiguity checklist**: After drafting any important provision, ask:
- Can this provision be read in two ways?
- If it can, is the intended reading clearly preferred by context and defined terms?
- If not, restructure until only one reading is plausible.

## Audience Awareness

Legal writing is always addressed to a specific audience. The audience determines the appropriate level of technical detail, the formality of language, the assumed level of legal knowledge, and the structure of the presentation.

### Primary Audiences

| Audience | Characteristics | Implications for Writing |
|----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Court (judge) | Legally trained; time-constrained; must decide on law and fact; accountable to appellate review | Use correct legal terminology; be concise; cite authority; follow court format rules; front-load key arguments |
| Client (non-lawyer) | Not legally trained; needs to make informed decisions; worried about risk and cost | Translate legal concepts; explain practical implications; use plain language; be clear about uncertainty |
| Client (in-house counsel) | Legally trained but may not be a specialist in this area | Use legal terminology but explain specialized concepts; focus on practical application |
| Supervising lawyer / partner | Legally trained; time-constrained; responsible for quality control | Structured, analytical; front-load conclusions (CREAC); flag issues clearly; use headings for navigation |
| Opposing counsel | Legally trained; adversarial relationship; will scrutinize the document for concessions and errors | Formal tone; precise language; do not concede unnecessarily; mark "without prejudice" where applicable |
| Regulator | Legally trained; investigating or assessing compliance; carries investigative powers | Responsive to the regulator's framing; factual accuracy is paramount; cooperative tone unless advised otherwise |
| Parliament / legislature | Not necessarily legally trained; policy-focused; requires explanatory material | Explanatory memoranda and second reading speeches must be accessible to non-lawyers |

### The Test of the Reasonable Reader

Courts interpret documents (particularly contracts and legislation) from the perspective of a "reasonable reader" --- a person with the background knowledge that the document's intended audience would bring to it, reading the document as a whole, without access to the writer's subjective intentions.

This test has a direct implication for legal drafting: write so that a reasonable reader in the intended audience would understand the document in the way you intend, without needing to know what was in your mind when you drafted it. The document must work independently of its author.

## Active vs Passive Voice

### Active Voice (the Default)

In active voice, the subject performs the action: "The board approved the resolution." Active voice is preferred in legal writing because it:
- Identifies the actor: the reader knows who must do what
- Is shorter: fewer words required
- Is clearer: the cause-and-effect structure is transparent
- Creates accountability: passive voice can obscure responsibility

### Passive Voice (Appropriate in Specific Contexts)

In passive voice, the subject receives the action: "The resolution was approved by the board."

Passive voice is appropriate when:
- The actor is genuinely unknown: "The server was accessed by an unknown third party."
- The actor is unimportant: "The application must be filed within 30 days." (The filing party is whoever is reading the rule; specifying them is unnecessary.)
- The focus should be on the recipient of the action: "The plaintiff was struck by the defendant's vehicle as she crossed the road."
- In legislation, where the obligation is imposed without naming a specific actor: "Written notice must be given."

The problem with passive voice in legal writing is not that it is technically incorrect. It is that it is frequently used to obscure who is responsible, which defeats the accountability function of legal documents.

**The 80/20 rule**: Aim for approximately 80% active constructions and 20% passive. This ratio produces writing that is predominantly clear and direct while permitting passive where it genuinely serves the communication.

## Sentence Structure

### Length Guidelines

| Document Type | Recommended Average | Practical Maximum |
|--------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Client advice letters (non-lawyer) | 15--20 words | 30 words |
| Legal memoranda | 20--25 words | 40 words |
| Court submissions | 20--25 words | 40 words |
| Legislation | 25--30 words (varies by drafting office) | 50 words |
| Commercial contracts | 20--25 words | 40 words |

### Structural Techniques

**Subject-verb-object first**: Place the main clause at the beginning of the sentence. Introduce qualifications and conditions after the main proposition.

*Before*: "Except in cases of fraud, negligent misrepresentation, or willful misconduct, and subject to the limitation period set out in clause 15, the party against whom a claim is made shall not be liable for any indirect, consequential, or special damages."

*After*: "The party against whom a claim is made is not liable for indirect, consequential, or special damages. Exceptions apply for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, and willful misconduct. Claims are subject to the limitation period in clause 15."

**Lists for multiple items**: When a provision contains three or more discrete items, use a formatted list:
> The board may, by resolution, authorize the company to:
> (a) borrow money;
> (b) issue debentures;
> (c) charge the company's property as security; and
> (d) give financial assistance to associated entities.

**Tabulation for conditions**: Conditional provisions with multiple alternatives are clearer when tabulated:
> The court may grant a mandatory injunction if:
> (a) the plaintiff establishes that a legal right has been infringed;
> (b) damages would not be an adequate remedy; and
> (c) the balance of convenience favors the injunction.

## Gender-Neutral Drafting

Gender-neutral drafting is now mandatory or strongly recommended in all six Codex jurisdictions. The use of "he" as the default pronoun for an unidentified person is both stylistically outdated and legally problematic in jurisdictions that have eliminated gender-specific pronouns from legislative drafting guidelines.

### Techniques

| Technique | Example | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Plural forms | "Employees must submit their timesheets" instead of "An employee must submit his timesheet" | The most natural and widely used technique |
| Singular "they" | "A party who wishes to appeal must file their notice within 28 days" | Now accepted in all major jurisdictions' drafting guides |
| Repeat the noun | "The applicant must ensure that the applicant's address is current" | Acceptable but becomes awkward with repeated use |
| Restructure to eliminate the pronoun | "The notice must be filed within 28 days" | Often the most elegant solution |
| Role or office title | "The chairperson" rather than "the chairman" | Use inclusive titles |

### Legislative Requirements by Jurisdiction

- **Australia**: The Office of Parliamentary Counsel guidelines require gender-neutral drafting for all Commonwealth legislation. The *Acts Interpretation Act 1901* (Cth) s 23 provides that words importing one gender include every other gender, but this is a remedial provision, not a substitute for gender-neutral drafting.
- **United Kingdom**: The Office of Parliamentary Counsel *Drafting Guidance* (2020) requires gender-neutral language in all government bills.
- **Canada**: The Department of Justice drafting conventions require gender-neutral language in both English and French drafts.
- **New Zealand**: The Parliamentary Counsel Office drafting manual requires gender-neutral language.
- **United States**: No federal statutory requirement, but the Plain Writing Act and agency-specific style guides increasingly adopt gender-neutral conventions. Many federal and state courts have adopted gender-neutral practice directions.

## Writing for Different Audiences: Practical Guidelines

### Writing for the Court

1. Follow the court's practice direction or style guide. Many courts publish specific formatting and citation requirements.
2. Use the court's required citation system (AGLC4 for most Australian courts; Bluebook for US federal courts; OSCOLA for UK courts).
3. Comply strictly with word limits, page limits, and filing deadlines. Non-compliance may result in rejection of the document.
4. Address the court correctly: "Your Honour" for Australian judges; "Your Lordship/Ladyship" for UK Supreme Court and senior UK judges; "Your Honor" in the US; "Your Honour" in Canada and NZ.
5. Front-load the key arguments. Judges read many submissions and must grasp the argument quickly. State your best point first.
6. Do not make arguments you cannot support. Courts remember lawyers who overstate their case.

### Writing for Clients

1. Translate every legal concept into its practical consequence. A non-lawyer client does not need a doctrinal explanation of negligence; they need to know whether they face a risk of liability and, if so, what the risk is.
2. Explain the practical implications: What will happen? When? At what cost? What are the alternatives?
3. Frame risk in terms the client can use for decision-making. Provide a probability estimate (see the risk framing methodology in [03-letters-of-advice.md](03-letters-of-advice.md)).
4. Be clear about what is certain, what is probable, and what is uncertain.
5. Avoid unnecessary qualifications that obscure the bottom line. If the answer is "yes," say "yes" and then explain the conditions.

### Writing for Opposing Counsel

1. Maintain professional courtesy throughout. Personal attacks and unprofessional language reflect badly on the writer and rarely achieve any strategic advantage.
2. Mark "without prejudice" (or "without prejudice save as to costs" where a *Calderbank* offer is intended) on settlement correspondence, as appropriate.
3. Be precise. Ambiguity in correspondence with opposing counsel will be exploited.
4. Keep a clear record. File all correspondence from and to opposing counsel in chronological order.

### Writing for Regulators

1. Be responsive to the exact questions asked. Do not provide information beyond what is asked unless there is a strategic reason to do so (and your supervising lawyer has cleared it).
2. Factual accuracy is paramount. A factual misstatement to a regulator can constitute an offense under some regulatory regimes.
3. Maintain legal professional privilege where it applies. Label privileged documents as such. Do not produce privileged documents unless specifically required and the privilege has been formally waived.
4. Keep tone cooperative but not apologetic. Do not make admissions that are not supported by the evidence.

## Common Writing Errors

| Error | Description | Solution |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Legalese | Using archaic or unnecessarily complex language for formality's sake | Apply plain language principles; use common words |
| Nominalizations | Turning verbs into nouns: "make a decision" instead of "decide" | Use the verb form |
| Throat-clearing | Long introductory phrases before the main point: "It should be noted that..." | Delete the intro; state the point directly |
| Buried verbs | Placing the main verb too far from the subject | Restructure: subject + verb + object |
| Over-qualification | Hedging every statement to the point of uselessness | State a reasoned assessment; use precise probability language |
| String citations | Citing five cases for a settled proposition that requires one | Cite the leading authority; add one recent application |
| Arguing in facts sections | Including legal argument in the Statement of Facts | Separate facts from analysis strictly |
| Failing to revise | Submitting a first draft | All legal writing requires at least one complete revision; preferably more |
