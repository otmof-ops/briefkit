# Legal Memoranda

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-02
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf`](columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf), [`precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf`](precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf)

The legal memorandum is the workhorse document of legal practice. It communicates the results of legal research and analysis to an internal audience --- a supervising lawyer, a partner, general counsel, or a senior team member. It is an objective document: it presents the law as it is, not as the writer or client wishes it to be. Effective memoranda enable sound decision-making by identifying the legal issues, stating the applicable law accurately, applying the law to the facts, and stating defensible conclusions.

This document covers memorandum structure, the practical application of IRAC methodology, issue identification and framing, rule synthesis, multi-issue analysis, formatting conventions, and the writing patterns that distinguish strong memoranda from weak ones.

---

## The Relationship Between Research and Writing

A memorandum is the written product of legal research. But the relationship between research and writing is iterative, not sequential. The writer begins with a question, conducts initial research, identifies the relevant legal framework, refines the question based on what the research reveals, conducts further targeted research, and then writes the analysis. The writing itself often exposes gaps in the research that require further investigation.

This means:
- **Begin writing early**: Draft the issue statement, brief answer, and facts section before the research is complete. This structures the research by forcing you to define what you are looking for.
- **Use writing to test the research**: If you cannot write a complete application section without hedging every sentence ("if the court adopts this approach..."), you need more research.
- **Revise the rule statement as you write**: Drafting the application section often reveals that the rule as initially stated does not cleanly map to the facts. Revise the rule statement to match what you actually found in the cases.
- **Do a final research sweep after drafting**: Use the draft memo as a research checklist. Every proposition cited in the application section should have verified authority.

The research memorandum writing method is addressed in detail in [../legal-research-and-methodology/07-research-memorandum-writing.md](../legal-research-and-methodology/07-research-memorandum-writing.md). This document focuses on the practical execution --- how to produce a well-written memo.

## Memorandum Structure

A standard legal memorandum contains the following components in the following order:

### 1. Header

```
MEMORANDUM

TO:       [Recipient name, title, and firm or organization]
FROM:     [Author name and title]
DATE:     [Date of memorandum]
RE:       [Client name / File or matter number]
SUBJECT:  [Brief, informative description of the legal question]

PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL --- LEGAL PROFESSIONAL PRIVILEGE
This memorandum is prepared for the purposes of legal advice. Do not copy or distribute without authorization.
```

The privilege notation should appear on every memorandum. This protects the document from production in litigation or regulatory investigation.

### 2. Question(s) Presented

A concise statement of each legal question, grounded in the facts of the matter. If there are multiple questions, number them. Each question should:
- Be a question (not a topic heading)
- Name the relevant parties or relationships
- Include enough factual context to connect the legal question to the specific matter
- Be specific enough to direct the analysis

The question presented is not: "Negligence." It is: "Whether Acme Corp owes a duty of care to Ms. Chen as the ultimate consumer of a widget manufactured by Acme and sold in sealed packaging through a retail chain."

### 3. Brief Answer(s)

A concise answer (one to three sentences) for each question presented. The brief answer should:
- State the conclusion directly
- Give the key reason
- Note any material qualification

> 1. Probably yes. A product manufacturer owes a duty of care to the ultimate consumer under *Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562. The main contested point is whether the sealed packaging satisfies the "no reasonable possibility of intermediate examination" element; the weight of authority suggests it does.

The brief answer enables the busy reader to grasp the position immediately before reading the analysis. If the entire memorandum is read, the brief answer is confirmed and expanded. If only the brief answer is read (by a client or partner who needs a quick answer), it states the position accurately.

### 4. Statement of Facts

A neutral, objective recitation of the relevant facts. The facts section:
- Includes all facts relevant to the legal analysis
- Includes adverse facts (do not omit unfavorable facts)
- Labels assumptions as assumptions
- Labels disputed facts as disputed
- Contains no legal analysis or argument
- Is organized chronologically (usually)

The facts section is not an advocacy document. It is the factual baseline for the analysis. Any facts relied upon in the Discussion section must appear in the Statement of Facts. Any fact that the Discussion omits (because it is unfavorable) will be noticed by the supervising lawyer, who will ask why it was not addressed.

**Typical opening sentences**:
> Our advice is based on the following facts as provided by [client/instructing lawyer] on [date]. Please advise us if any of these facts are incorrect or if additional facts are available, as the analysis may change.

### 5. Discussion

The substantive analytical section. This is where IRAC or CREAC analysis is applied to each issue. See the section below on IRAC in practice.

### 6. Conclusion

A consolidated summary of the conclusions on all issues. For a single-issue memo, this may be one or two sentences that restate the conclusion from the Discussion. For a multi-issue memo, this provides a "big picture" view:

> In summary:
> 1. On Issue 1 (duty of care): Acme Corp likely owes Ms. Chen a duty of care.
> 2. On Issue 2 (breach): If a duty exists, the failure to inspect the widget before sealing it constitutes a breach.
> 3. On Issue 3 (causation): Causation is likely established given the direct link between the defect and the injury.
> 4. On Issue 4 (damages): Ms. Chen's recoverable damages include her economic loss and medical expenses, but not her claim for distress (which requires a recognized psychiatric injury).

### 7. Qualifications and Limitations

State the scope and limits of the memorandum:
- The date on which the advice is current
- What questions are addressed and what questions are not
- Any assumptions that underlie the analysis
- Any areas where the law is unsettled

## IRAC Applied in Practice

### Issue Identification and Framing

Issue identification is the most important step in writing a legal memorandum. A correctly framed issue directs the entire analysis. A poorly framed issue --- too broad, too narrow, or wrongly classified --- produces analysis that misses the point or answers the wrong question.

**Technique 1: Work backward from the desired legal outcome**

1. What does the client want to achieve (or avoid)?
2. What legal requirements must be satisfied (or blocked) for that outcome?
3. For each legal requirement, is there a genuine question about whether it is satisfied on the facts?
4. Each such genuine question is an issue.

**Technique 2: Element decomposition**

1. Identify the cause of action, statutory provision, or defense that is potentially engaged.
2. Break it into its legal elements (from the case law or statute).
3. For each element, ask: is this element genuinely contested on the facts of this matter?
4. Elements that are not contested (because the facts clearly satisfy them or clearly fail to satisfy them) are not issues.
5. Elements that are genuinely contested are issues.

**Example: Negligent misstatement claim**

Elements (derived from *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465 (HL); *Shaddock & Associates Pty Ltd v Parramatta City Council* (1981) 150 CLR 225 (HCA)):
1. The defendant made a statement
2. The statement was false or misleading
3. The defendant owed a duty of care to the plaintiff
4. The plaintiff relied on the statement
5. The reliance was reasonable
6. The plaintiff suffered loss as a result of the reliance

Suppose elements 1, 2, 4, and 6 are clear on the facts (there was a statement, it was wrong, the plaintiff acted on it, they lost money). The issues are:
- Issue 1: Did the defendant owe a duty of care? (Is there a "special relationship"?)
- Issue 2: Was the plaintiff's reliance reasonable? (Did the disclaimer negate reasonableness?)

These two elements are the genuine questions. The memo focuses on them.

### Rule Synthesis from Multiple Authorities

Rule synthesis is the process of integrating multiple authorities into a coherent statement of the governing rule. It is one of the most important skills in memo writing.

**The synthesis imperative**: Legal rules are rarely found in a single authoritative source. They develop across multiple decisions over time. Each case adds a dimension: a new application, a refinement, a limitation, an exception. The researcher's job is to weave these into a coherent statement of the current rule.

**Poor synthesis (case listing)**:
> In *Case A*, the court held X. In *Case B*, the court held Y. In *Case C*, the court held Z.

This is not synthesis. The reader must construct the rule from the three summaries.

**Good synthesis**:
> The test for [element] requires [formulation of synthesized rule]: *Case A* [year] [citation] (court). The rule extends to [circumstance A]: *Case B* [citation]. It does not apply to [circumstance B]: *Case C* [citation]. Where [circumstance C], the approach in *Case A* is modified to [formulation]: *Case C* [citation] ([judge]).

**Building a synthesis**:

1. Read all the relevant cases on the point.
2. Identify the proposition(s) for which each case is authority.
3. Identify how the cases relate to each other: Does Case B follow Case A? Narrow it? Extend it? Qualify it?
4. Draft a sentence (or paragraph) that states the general rule and cross-references all the cases, with each case cited for the specific proposition it supports.
5. Test the synthesis by asking: if someone only read the synthesis (and not the cases), would they have an accurate understanding of the current state of the law?

### Application to Facts

The application section is where the memo's analytical value is realized. Stating the rule correctly is necessary; applying it to the specific facts with precision and rigor is the skill that distinguishes strong from weak legal analysis.

**Weak application pattern (assertion)**:
> On the facts, the defendant owed a duty of care.

**Strong application pattern (analysis)**:
> The defendant owed a duty of care for the following reasons. First, [specific fact A] establishes the [element]: cf *Authority*, where [analogous fact]. Second, [specific fact B] establishes [element]: the defendant [did/said/represented], which satisfies the requirement in *Authority* that the defendant [formulation]. The only contested point is [element X]. The defendant would argue that [counter-argument]. This argument [is unlikely to succeed / has some force] because [analysis].

**The comparison imperative**: In the application section, you must compare the facts of your matter to the facts of the authorities. "The facts are analogous to *Case X*" without explaining the analogy is insufficient. State the parallel: "The sealed packaging in the present matter is analogous to the sealed bottle in *Grant v Australian Knitting Mills* [1936] AC 85, where the court held that the absence of intermediate inspection between the manufacturer and the consumer satisfied the [element]."

### Addressing Counter-Arguments

An objective memorandum must address the strongest arguments on the other side. This is not just intellectual honesty; it is practical necessity. The supervising lawyer needs to know the risks, not just the strengths.

**Technique**:

1. State the client's likely strongest position on the element.
2. "The opposing party would argue that..." (state the best counter-argument)
3. "This argument [is likely to succeed / is unlikely to succeed / has some force but is outweighed] because..."
4. If the counter-argument has real merit: "This argument has force. The outcome on this element is genuinely uncertain. On balance, we assess that..."

Never ignore a strong counter-argument. Courts will notice it; opposing counsel will use it; the supervising lawyer needs to assess it.

## Multiple Issues and Sub-Issues

Complex memoranda address multiple issues, often with sub-issues. Organizing multi-issue analysis requires both logical structure and visual clarity.

### Organizing Multi-Issue Memos

**Logical ordering principles**:

- **Threshold issues first**: Address issues whose resolution determines whether other issues arise. If there is no duty of care, the questions of breach, causation, and damage do not need to be fully analyzed. Address the duty issue first and state the assumption under which the others are analyzed.
- **Independent issues in logical order**: For issues that do not depend on each other, address them in an order that is clear to the reader: typically from most important to least important, or from most likely to be dispositive to least likely.
- **Interdependent issues with decision trees**: Where the resolution of Issue 1 produces different Issue 2 analyses, structure it as: "If Issue 1 is resolved in [way], the analysis of Issue 2 is [A]. If Issue 1 is resolved in [different way], the analysis of Issue 2 is [B]."

**Heading hierarchy**:

For a multi-issue memo with sub-issues:

```
DISCUSSION

I.   WHETHER ACME CORP OWES A DUTY OF CARE TO MS. CHEN

     A. The Test for Duty of Care in Product Liability
     B. Assumption of Responsibility
     C. The "No Reasonable Possibility of Intermediate Examination" Element
     D. Effect of the Disclaimer

II.  IF A DUTY EXISTS, WHETHER IT WAS BREACHED

     A. The Standard of Care for a Product Manufacturer
     B. Whether Acme Corp's Conduct Fell Below That Standard

III. CAUSATION AND DAMAGE

     A. Whether the Breach Caused the Loss
     B. Remoteness
```

This structure gives the reader a roadmap before the analysis begins and enables them to navigate directly to the section most relevant to their immediate need.

### Issue-Outcome Matrix for Complex Memos

For memoranda with many interdependent issues, an issue-outcome matrix at the beginning of the Discussion section (or in the Conclusion) helps the reader understand the overall position:

| Issue | Conclusion | Confidence | Dispositive? |
|-------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Duty of care | Probably yes | 70% | Yes --- no other issues arise if no duty |
| Breach | Probably yes | 75% | Yes --- if duty established |
| Causation | Probable | 65% | Yes --- if breach established |
| Remoteness of damage (psychiatric injury) | Probably no | 70% | Limits damages if established |
| Limitation period | Not expired | 90% | No --- does not defeat the claim |

**Overall assessment**: On balance, Ms. Chen has a viable negligence claim against Acme Corp. The main risk is the remoteness issue for the psychiatric injury component.

## Memorandum Formatting Conventions

### Length

| Type | Typical Length | Context |
|------|---------------|---------|
| Short-form memo (single issue) | 2--5 pages | Quick question from supervising lawyer |
| Standard memo (2--4 issues) | 5--15 pages | Research assignment on a discrete legal question |
| Comprehensive memo (5+ issues) | 15--40 pages | Major transaction, litigation strategy, regulatory compliance program |

The rule: include everything necessary, omit everything else. Padding with background the reader already knows, repeating the rule in the application section, and over-qualifying settled propositions all add length without adding value.

### Citation Practice

Follow the citation system of the relevant jurisdiction. See [../legal-research-and-methodology/05-legal-citation-systems.md](../legal-research-and-methodology/05-legal-citation-systems.md).

Common citation practice in memos:
- **Australian memos**: AGLC4; footnotes or in-text depending on firm practice
- **US memos**: Bluebook (practitioners' rules); in-text citations with parenthetical brief descriptions
- **UK memos**: OSCOLA; footnotes
- **Canadian memos**: McGill Guide; often in-text with footnotes for longer citations

Use consistent citation format throughout. Do not mix Bluebook and OSCOLA conventions in the same document.

### Headings and Typography

Use descriptive headings that communicate the content of each section. "Section 3" is not a heading; "Whether the Plaintiff Assumed the Risk" is.

Typical heading hierarchy:
- **Level 1**: UPPERCASE BOLD (DISCUSSION, CONCLUSION)
- **Level 2**: **Title Case Bold** (Issue headings)
- **Level 3**: *Title Case Italic* (Sub-issue headings)
- **Level 4**: Sentence case underline (detailed analytical points)

Consistent heading levels signal the structure to the reader. Inconsistent heading levels produce a document that is disorienting to navigate.

## Practice Examples Across Practice Areas

### Contract Law Issue

> **Question Presented**: Whether the email exchange between the parties on 15 March 2026 constitutes a binding contract for the sale of 123 Main Street for $2.5 million, where no formal contract has been executed.
>
> **Brief Answer**: Probably not, on its own. In New South Wales, a contract for the sale of land must be evidenced in writing signed by the party against whom it is sought to be enforced: *Conveyancing Act 1919* (NSW) s 54A. An email may constitute written evidence (*Stellard Pty Ltd v North Queensland Fuel Pty Ltd* [2015] QSC 119), but only if the emails together contain all essential terms (parties, property, price, and any applicable special conditions) and the email signature constitutes a "signing." On the present facts, the emails identify the parties, property, and price, but do not address the standard vendor terms required by the *Conveyancing Act 1919* (NSW). Further analysis is required to determine whether the statutory writing requirement can be satisfied.

### Tort Law Issue

> **Question Presented**: Whether the employer is vicariously liable for the employee's assault on a customer during a heated dispute over a product return at the service counter.
>
> **Brief Answer**: Possibly. Vicarious liability extends to intentional torts where the tort is sufficiently connected to the employment: *Prince Alfred College Inc v ADC* (2016) 258 CLR 134, [80]. The test is whether the employment created the occasion or materially increased the risk for the tort. The connection between the employee's role (managing customer returns) and the conduct (assault during a return dispute) may satisfy the test; however, a purely personal motivation for the assault would be a defense. The analysis is highly fact-specific.

### Regulatory Compliance Issue

> **Question Presented**: Whether the company's proposed practice of collecting customer location data in its mobile application complies with the notifiable data breach scheme under Part IIIC of the *Privacy Act 1988* (Cth).
>
> **Brief Answer**: The proposed practice does not itself engage the notifiable data breach scheme. The scheme is triggered by an "eligible data breach" --- an unauthorized disclosure or loss of personal information that is likely to result in serious harm: *Privacy Act 1988* (Cth) s 26WA. The mere collection of location data is not an eligible data breach. However, if the location data is subsequently subject to unauthorized access or disclosure, the notification obligations in ss 26WK--26WR would apply. The company should implement data security measures consistent with APP 11 to minimize the risk of a notifiable data breach occurring.
