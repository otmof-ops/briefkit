# Letters of Advice

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-03
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf`](columbia-law-clear-active-legal-writing.pdf), [`precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf`](precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf), [`persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf`](persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf)

A letter of advice (or opinion letter) communicates legal analysis and recommendations to a client. Unlike a research memorandum, which is typically addressed to another lawyer, a letter of advice is addressed to a person who may have limited or no legal training. The writer must translate legal analysis into language the client can use to make informed decisions, while maintaining professional accuracy and managing professional liability risk.

This document covers the structure of advice letters, techniques for communicating risk to non-lawyer clients, privilege and confidentiality considerations, professional liability awareness, and the conventions for specific types of opinion letters.

---

## The Purpose of a Letter of Advice

A letter of advice performs several distinct functions simultaneously:

1. **Answers the client's legal question**: The client has asked a specific question. The letter answers it clearly and accurately.
2. **Enables decision-making**: The client must be able to use the advice to make informed decisions about their commercial, personal, or legal affairs. If the client cannot understand the advice or does not know what to do with it, the letter has failed.
3. **Creates a file record**: The letter documents the advice given, which is relevant to questions of professional liability, the running of limitation periods, and the scope of the retainer.
4. **Defines scope**: The letter states what questions it addresses and, where relevant, what questions it deliberately does not address. Scope definition protects the practitioner from claims that the advice covered matters it did not.
5. **Discharges professional obligations**: In some jurisdictions and circumstances, a lawyer has a positive obligation to advise on certain matters. The advice letter demonstrates that the obligation has been discharged.

## Structure of an Advice Letter

### 1. Letterhead and Reference

Include:
- Firm letterhead with contact details
- Date
- Client's name and address
- File or matter reference number
- A brief subject line identifying the matter

### 2. Salutation and Opening

Begin with a professional salutation and a brief opening that confirms the instructions received and states the purpose of the letter.

> Dear Ms. Chen,
>
> Thank you for your instructions of 10 March 2026 regarding the proposed acquisition of 123 Main Street, Springfield. You have asked us to advise on two questions:
> 1. Whether the existing lease to Tenant Co. survives the sale of the property to you.
> 2. What rights you would have to terminate the lease after settlement.
>
> We set out our advice below.

This opening:
- Confirms the instructions (and gives the client an opportunity to correct any misunderstanding)
- States the questions addressed (defining scope from the outset)
- Sets the professional tone

### 3. Summary of Advice

Provide the key conclusions up front. The client needs the answer before the analysis. Most clients read the summary and then decide how much of the detail to read.

> **In summary:**
>
> 1. The lease will survive the sale. When you purchase the property, you will be bound by the existing lease on its current terms for the remainder of the lease term.
>
> 2. Your rights to terminate the lease are limited. You can terminate only in the circumstances provided for in the lease itself --- primarily, if the tenant breaches the lease. You cannot terminate simply because you wish to use the property for a different purpose. The lease expires on 30 June 2028.

This summary:
- Gives the bottom line clearly
- Uses plain language
- States a consequence, not just a legal rule
- Omits citations (which go in the detailed analysis if needed)

### 4. Background / Facts Relied Upon

State the facts on which the advice is based. This section:
- Confirms your understanding of the client's instructions
- Gives the client an opportunity to correct factual errors
- Establishes the scope of the advice (the advice is premised on these facts; if facts change, the advice may change)

> **Facts relied upon**
>
> Our advice is based on the following facts as provided by you on 10 March 2026:
> - The property is a commercial premises at 123 Main Street, Springfield, New South Wales.
> - Tenant Co. holds a registered lease expiring 30 June 2028 at a current rent of $120,000 per annum.
> - The lease contains a standard break clause allowing termination for breach with 14 days' written notice and an opportunity for the tenant to cure.
> - The purchase price is $2.5 million.
> - Settlement is scheduled for 30 April 2026.
> - [Any assumptions: "We assume that the lease has been registered on the title as stated."]
>
> Please advise us if any of these facts are incorrect or if additional information is available, as our advice may change.

### 5. Detailed Analysis

Present the legal analysis in plain language. Use headings for each issue. Cite the relevant law but explain what it means in practical terms --- the translation technique.

**The translation technique**:

The key difference between a memo and a letter of advice is the translation requirement. In a memo written to a supervising lawyer, you can write: "Under s 117 of the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW), a registered lease runs with the land and binds successors in title." In an advice letter, that statement needs to become:

> When a lease is registered on the title of the property --- as this lease is --- the law requires that the new owner step into the shoes of the previous owner and honor the lease on its existing terms. The registration of the lease gives it a form of legal protection that means your purchase does not extinguish it. This is established by section 117 of the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW), which has been confirmed by the courts, including the High Court, in a number of property transactions of this kind.

**What the translation does**:
- Uses "the law requires" instead of a statutory citation as the operative phrase
- Explains the practical consequence ("step into the shoes of the previous owner")
- Gives the client the "why" (the registration gives legal protection)
- Includes the legal source for completeness, but makes it secondary to the explanation

**Structure of the analysis section**:

Use a heading for each issue addressed. Under each heading:
1. State the key legal rule in plain language
2. Apply it to the client's specific facts
3. Identify any risk or uncertainty
4. State the practical consequence

Do not write the analysis section the way you would write a research memo. The audience is different.

### 6. Risk Assessment

Clients need to understand not just the legal position but their degree of confidence in it and the consequences of different outcomes.

**Risk framing framework**:

Frame risk on two dimensions: **probability** (how likely is a particular outcome?) and **consequence** (what happens if that outcome occurs?).

**Probability language:**
- "It is almost certain that..." (greater than 90% confidence)
- "It is very likely that..." (75--90%)
- "It is likely that..." (60--75%)
- "There is a reasonable possibility that..." (40--60%)
- "It is possible but uncertain whether..." (25--40%)
- "It is unlikely that..." (10--25%)
- "It is very unlikely that..." (less than 10%)

**Consequence framing:**
- Financial exposure: quantify in dollar amounts where possible
- Timing: when might the consequence materialize?
- Operational impact: business disruption, delays, forced exit
- Regulatory risk: investigation, enforcement, fines
- Reputational impact (where relevant)

**Example risk framing**:

> **Rent Review Risk**
>
> There is a moderate risk (we assess it at approximately 40--45%) that the tenant would argue successfully that the rent review clause in the lease entitles it to a downward rent review at the next review date (1 July 2026).
>
> If this argument succeeded, the annual rent could reduce by approximately $15,000--$25,000 (based on current market comparables for similar premises) for the approximately 24-month remaining term. This represents a potential reduction in rental income of $30,000--$50,000 over the remaining term.
>
> The reason for the uncertainty is that the rent review clause uses the phrase "current market rent," which has been interpreted inconsistently in New South Wales courts depending on how comparable properties are defined. There is no case directly on point with premises of this type.

This risk framing:
- States the probability in clear, graduated language
- Quantifies the financial consequence
- Explains the source of the uncertainty
- Does not pretend certainty where it does not exist
- Does not refuse to give an assessment because of the uncertainty

### 7. Options and Recommendations

Where appropriate, present the client's practical options and the firm's recommendation. Structure options as distinct alternatives with a clear description, the advantages and disadvantages of each, and a recommendation.

> **Options**
>
> You have three main options:
>
> **Option A: Proceed on current terms.** Proceed with the acquisition without seeking to negotiate the rent review clause. This accepts the risk of a downward rent review. This may be appropriate if the purchase price has been negotiated to account for the risk.
>
> **Option B: Seek a rent review deed of variation.** Before settlement, negotiate with the seller to facilitate a deed of variation with the tenant that clarifies the rent review mechanism. This eliminates the rent review ambiguity but requires the tenant's agreement.
>
> **Option C: Withdraw from the acquisition.** Decline to proceed and forfeit the deposit (approximately $62,500, being 2.5% of the purchase price). This eliminates all property risk but forfeits the deposit and the commercial opportunity.
>
> **Our recommendation**: We recommend Option B. We suggest that your real estate agent contact the tenant before settlement to explore a deed of variation. If the tenant agrees, we can draft the deed. If the tenant refuses, Option A is acceptable, provided the purchase price reflects the risk. We do not recommend Option C unless you have other commercial reasons for withdrawing.

### 8. Scope and Qualifications

State what the advice does and does not cover.

> **Scope of this advice**
>
> This advice:
> - Addresses only the two questions stated above.
> - Is current as at 10 March 2026.
> - Is based on the law of New South Wales. If the property were in another state, the analysis could differ.
> - Does not address tax implications (we recommend you consult your accountant before settlement).
> - Does not address planning or environmental compliance (a separate review would be required if you intend to alter the property's use).
> - Assumes the facts provided to us are complete and accurate.

### 9. Invitation to Contact

Conclude by inviting the client to contact you with questions and asking them to confirm how they wish to proceed.

> We are happy to discuss any aspect of this advice with you. Please contact [name] on [phone/email] if you have questions.
>
> Please let us know how you wish to proceed, and we will take the necessary steps.
>
> Yours sincerely,
>
> [Signature block]

## Communicating Risk to Non-Lawyer Clients

The most common failure in advice letters is over-qualification. A letter that says "it depends" or "the outcome is uncertain" without providing a reasoned assessment is professionally inadequate. Clients cannot make decisions on the basis of "it depends." They need an assessment of the most likely outcome, together with an explanation of the uncertainty.

### The Failure Mode: Defensive Advising

Defensive advising is the practice of hedging every statement so thoroughly that no useful information is conveyed. It is driven by the fear of being wrong and the misguided belief that refusing to give a view protects the lawyer from liability.

**Defensive advice** (inadequate):
> "The outcome in this matter is uncertain. The courts have not definitively resolved the question. We recommend you seek further advice and consider your options carefully before proceeding."

**Useful advice** (adequate):
> "On balance, we assess it as more likely than not (approximately 65%) that a court would find in your favor on this issue. The main risk is the defendant's argument regarding the disclaimer clause. We believe this argument is unlikely to succeed because [reasons], but it cannot be dismissed. If the argument succeeded, your damages claim would be reduced from approximately $200,000 to approximately $80,000. We recommend proceeding with the litigation on this basis."

The second approach gives the client: a probability estimate, an identification of the main risk, a basis for that assessment, a quantification of the downside, and a recommendation. The client can make a decision.

### The Principle of Reasoned Uncertainty

The appropriate standard for legal advice is not certainty. Legal certainty is rare. The appropriate standard is **reasoned uncertainty**: the lawyer identifies the most likely outcome, explains the basis for that view, and identifies the material risks in descending order of probability and consequence.

A well-qualified piece of advice might say: "We assess that it is likely (70%) that you would succeed on liability. The main uncertainty is [issue], where the cases are divided. If the court follows [approach A], you would likely succeed; if it follows [approach B], you would not. On balance, [approach A] is better supported by the weight of authority."

This is honest, useful, and protective of both client and lawyer.

## Privilege Considerations

### Legal Professional Privilege

Communications between a lawyer and client for the dominant purpose of providing or receiving legal advice are protected by legal professional privilege (called attorney-client privilege in the US). Privileged communications:
- Cannot generally be compelled to be produced in legal proceedings or regulatory investigations
- Remain confidential even after the retainer ends
- Belong to the client (not the lawyer)

**For a letter of advice to be privileged**:
- It must be a communication between the lawyer and the client (or an agent of the client)
- The dominant purpose must be the provision of legal advice
- It must remain confidential

Mark advice letters:
```
PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL --- LEGAL PROFESSIONAL PRIVILEGE
```

### Waiver of Privilege

Privilege is waived if the client discloses the advice to a third party in circumstances inconsistent with maintaining confidentiality. Key risk areas:
- Sharing the advice letter with a counterparty or potential counterparty
- Using the advice letter in negotiations (which may constitute waiver depending on the extent of disclosure)
- Putting the advice letter on the record in litigation

Before the client shares the letter with a third party, advise them on the privilege implications.

**Dominant purpose test**: Not all lawyer-client communications are privileged. Communications that are primarily commercial or factual (rather than legal advice) may not satisfy the dominant purpose test. Mark documents carefully and do not assume that attaching a legal question to a commercial email makes the communication privileged.

## Professional Liability Considerations

The advice letter is the primary document against which a professional negligence claim will be assessed. In any negligence action against a lawyer, the question will be: did the lawyer provide advice that a reasonably competent practitioner in the same field would have provided?

### Key Risk Areas

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| Incorrect statement of law | Thorough research; citator checks; peer review before sending |
| Failure to identify a relevant issue | Comprehensive issue-spotting; consider the matter from multiple angles; use checklists for specific transaction types |
| Failure to consider adverse authority | Objective analysis; do not reason toward a preferred conclusion |
| Failure to update advice when the law changes | Set research alerts on key legislation and cases; note the date of the advice and the need to update if the law changes |
| Failure to adequately qualify uncertain advice | Use appropriate probability language; do not state certainty where it does not exist |
| Scope too narrow | Expressly state scope limitations; err on the side of completeness |
| Incorrect facts | Confirm the factual basis of the advice; label assumptions; ask the client to verify your factual summary |

### Updating Obligations

Whether a lawyer has a continuing obligation to update advice when the law changes after the letter is sent depends on the jurisdiction and the terms of the retainer. In practice:
- State clearly in the letter that it is current as at its date
- Advise the client to contact you if they are about to act on the advice, to confirm it remains current
- Where the matter is ongoing (not a one-off query), consider whether the retainer creates an implied updating obligation

## Specific Types of Advice Letters

### Due Diligence Opinion Letters

Due diligence opinions are provided in the context of transactions (mergers and acquisitions, real estate transactions, financing). They confirm that specified legal requirements have been satisfied or identify exceptions.

Key requirements:
- State the scope precisely (what has been reviewed; what has not)
- State the assumptions on which the opinion is based
- State the qualifications (limits on liability, reliance)
- Use standard formulations where they exist (e.g., legal capacity and authorization opinions follow standard formulations in most jurisdictions)

### Legal Capacity Opinions

A legal capacity opinion confirms that a party has the legal capacity to enter into a transaction (e.g., that a company has been duly incorporated, has the power to enter the agreement, and has taken the required corporate steps to authorize execution).

Standard structure:
1. We have been asked to opine on [specific capacity questions]
2. We have reviewed [documents reviewed]
3. We have assumed [standard assumptions: authenticity of documents, genuineness of signatures, etc.]
4. Based on the foregoing, we are of the opinion that [specific conclusions]
5. This opinion is limited to [jurisdiction]; we express no opinion on the laws of any other jurisdiction

### Letters of Comfort

A letter of comfort is a letter given in connection with a transaction (typically financing) that does not constitute a binding legal commitment but expresses the writer's position on specified matters. They are weaker than opinions but are used where the writer cannot or will not give a binding opinion.

Letters of comfort must be drafted with extreme care. The line between a letter of comfort and a contractual commitment is determined by the words used, and courts have sometimes treated comfort letters as binding: *Kleinwort Benson Ltd v Malaysia Mining Corp Bhd* [1989] 1 WLR 379 (CA) (finding that the letter in that case was not binding, but on facts where the language was carefully non-committal).
