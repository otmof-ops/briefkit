# Court Submissions

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-04
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf`](persuasive-legal-writing-guide.pdf), [`crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf`](crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf), [`uksc-practice-directions.pdf`](uksc-practice-directions.pdf)

Court submissions --- also called written submissions, briefs, skeleton arguments, or written arguments depending on the jurisdiction and court level --- are the primary vehicle for legal advocacy. Unlike research memoranda, which are objective, court submissions are persuasive. Their purpose is to convince a court to decide the case in the client's favor, within the constraints of professional ethics, evidentiary rules, and court procedure.

This document covers submission structure, persuasive writing technique, authority selection and presentation, dealing with adverse authority, oral submissions, and the varying requirements across courts and jurisdictions.

---

## The Nature of Persuasive Legal Writing

Persuasive legal writing differs from objective analysis in its purpose but not in its standards of accuracy. A lawyer may never:
- Make any false or misleading statement of fact or law to the court
- Cite a case that has been overruled without disclosing that fact
- Misstate the effect of a case or statute
- Conceal authority directly on point and adverse to the client's position

Within these constraints, the advocate's task is to present the facts, issues, and law in the manner most favorable to the client. This is not dishonesty; it is legitimate advocacy.

### The Three Modes of Persuasion

Legal persuasion draws on the classical rhetorical framework attributed to Aristotle:

**Logos (logic)**: The strength of the legal argument. Is the analysis internally consistent? Does the authority support the proposition? Is the application to the facts sound? Logos is the foundation: a legally flawed argument will not persuade a careful judge however well it is presented.

**Ethos (credibility)**: The advocate's reputation for accuracy, fairness, and competence. Courts are more receptive to arguments from advocates who have a track record of reliable, honest submissions. Ethos is built by: citing authority accurately and in context; acknowledging weaknesses before they are raised by the opponent; not overstating the strength of the case. The fastest way to lose ethos is to be caught overstating a case or mischaracterizing a case.

**Pathos (the equities)**: The sense that justice favors the client's position. Judges are not immune to the persuasive force of a compelling factual narrative. While courts decide on law and fact, the perception that justice is on a party's side influences the enthusiasm with which a court finds for them. The statement of facts is the primary vehicle for pathos.

### The Ethical Constraints on Persuasion

Professional responsibility rules in all six jurisdictions impose a duty to the court that qualifies the duty to the client. The duty to the court includes:
- Not making statements known to be false
- Not presenting witnesses or evidence known to be false
- Not suppressing authority directly on point that is adverse to the client's position (the duty of candor)
- Correcting any inadvertent misstatement once discovered

The duty to the court is not superseded by the duty of loyalty to the client. A lawyer who suppresses directly adverse authority may be disciplined, and in some jurisdictions this constitutes professional misconduct.

## Submission Structure

Most court submissions follow a common structure, with variations by court, jurisdiction, and the type of proceeding.

### Standard Structure

**1. Cover page** (if required by court rules)
- Court name
- Case name and number
- Parties (appellant/respondent; plaintiff/defendant)
- Title of document ("Written Submissions of the Appellant")
- Filing party's name, contact details, and solicitor reference
- Date of filing

**2. Table of contents** (for longer submissions)
Enable the reader to navigate directly to specific sections. Every submission over about 10 pages should have a table of contents.

**3. Summary of argument / Overview**
A concise statement (one to two paragraphs, or a short numbered list) of the key propositions. This section:
- Tells the court where the argument is going before the analysis begins
- Gives a busy judge the gist of the submission without reading every page
- Sets the framework for the detailed argument that follows

**4. Statement of issues**
The legal questions the court must resolve, stated in terms favorable to the submitting party.

**5. Statement of facts**
A narrative of the relevant facts drawn from the evidence in the record. Facts must be supported by references to the record (transcript, affidavit paragraphs, exhibit numbers).

**6. Argument**
The substantive legal analysis organized by issue. This is the body of the submission.

**7. Orders sought**
The specific orders the court is asked to make. Be precise: not "a judgment for the plaintiff" but "judgment for the plaintiff against the defendant in the sum of $500,000 plus costs on the ordinary basis."

**8. List of authorities**
A table of all cases, statutes, and secondary sources cited, with full citations. This is required by most court rules.

## The Statement of Facts

The statement of facts is often the most important section of a submission. Judges form their initial impression of the case from the facts, and the framing of the facts shapes how the legal issues are perceived.

### Principles for Persuasive Fact Presentation

**1. Be accurate**: Every factual assertion must be supported by evidence in the record. Cite the evidentiary source for each fact: "(Ms. Chen Affidavit, [12])" or "(Transcript, Day 2, 45:3--8)" or "(Exhibit P-7, page 3)." An unverified factual assertion invites challenge and undermines credibility.

**2. Be selective**: Include only facts that are relevant to the legal issues. Extraneous facts dilute the narrative and waste the reader's time.

**3. Lead with strong facts**: Present the facts most favorable to your client early. First impressions matter. The narrative arc you create should make the legal argument feel like the natural conclusion.

**4. Use chronological order** (generally): Chronological narratives are easier for the reader to follow. Depart from chronology only when thematic organization is clearly superior for the specific case.

**5. Let the facts do the work**: Do not argue in the facts section. Present the facts neutrally (using direct quotation from the evidence where the language is important) in a way that makes the argument seem obvious. The facts should create momentum toward the desired legal conclusion without needing advocacy language.

**The same facts, framed differently:**

*Plaintiff's framing*:
> On 15 January 2025, Ms. Chen engaged the defendant specifically to value 123 Main Street before she purchased it. She told the defendant she would rely on the valuation to decide whether to proceed. The defendant provided a written report on its professional letterhead, which Ms. Chen used to negotiate the purchase price. When the true value was discovered to be $300,000 less than the valuation, Ms. Chen had already settled.

*Defendant's framing*:
> The defendant is a registered valuer with 25 years' commercial property experience. Its engagement letter stated that the valuation was "for general guidance only" and "not a substitute for a full property inspection." Ms. Chen received this qualification before the valuation was delivered. She did not request a full inspection valuation before proceeding to settlement.

Both narratives are factually accurate. Each emphasizes different aspects of the same facts. Each primes the reader to reach a different conclusion about the legal issues.

## Framing Issues Favorably

The framing of legal issues significantly influences how they are resolved. An issue framed broadly may produce a different answer than the same issue framed narrowly.

**Plaintiff's issue framing**: "Whether a professional valuer owes a duty of care when it provides a written valuation report knowing the client will rely on it to decide whether to purchase a property."

**Defendant's issue framing**: "Whether a professional valuer who issues a report explicitly described as general guidance, and not a substitute for a full inspection, owes a duty of care to a client who chooses to rely on that report without obtaining a full inspection."

Both are legitimate framings of the same factual situation. The plaintiff's framing emphasizes the professional relationship and reliance. The defendant's framing emphasizes the disclaimer and the plaintiff's own choices. The court must resolve the issue however it is framed, but the framing primes the analysis.

**Technique**: When drafting the Statement of Issues, ask: "What question, if answered in our favor, would produce the outcome we seek?" That is the issue to frame.

## Authority Selection and Presentation

### Selecting Authorities

Select authorities that are:
1. **Binding on the court**: Decisions of superior courts in the same court hierarchy take priority
2. **Recent**: More recent decisions are generally preferred over older ones (unless an older decision is the leading case)
3. **Factually analogous**: The closer the factual similarity between the cited case and the present case, the more persuasive the authority
4. **From the same jurisdiction**: Same-jurisdiction authority is stronger than foreign authority (which is only persuasive)
5. **From respected courts or judges**: A decision by a judge renowned for expertise in the area carries additional persuasive force

### How Many Authorities to Cite

Less is more. Courts are not impressed by a list of fifteen cases cited for a single settled proposition. The list signals that the advocate cannot identify the most authoritative cases. Cite:
- The leading authority (typically from the highest court that has addressed the point)
- One or two recent decisions that apply the principle to factually analogous circumstances
- Additional authorities only where each adds something the leading cases do not

**String citations**: Avoid "See *Case A*; *Case B*; *Case C*; *Case D*" without explanation. Each citation should be meaningful; the court should understand what each case adds.

### Presenting Authorities in the Argument

When you present an authority, the judge needs to understand:
1. **What the case decided** (the proposition for which you cite it)
2. **What the relevant facts were** (briefly; the analogy depends on factual similarity)
3. **What the relevant passage says** (quote the key language if it is important)
4. **Why it supports your argument** (make the connection explicit)

**Weak authority presentation**:
> See *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465 (HL).

**Strong authority presentation**:
> In *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465 (HL), the House of Lords held that a "special relationship" between parties --- characterized by one party's assumption of responsibility for the accuracy of information and the other's reasonable reliance on it --- could give rise to a duty of care in negligent misstatement even in the absence of a contractual relationship: at 486 (Lord Reid). The present case falls squarely within this principle. Acme Corp was engaged specifically to provide a valuation on which Ms. Chen would rely; it issued a formal written report on its professional letterhead; and it knew that Ms. Chen would use the report to decide whether to proceed with the purchase.

The second approach explains the case, extracts the relevant principle, and draws the analogy. It does the work that persuasion requires.

## Dealing with Adverse Authority

### The Obligation to Disclose

In all six Codex jurisdictions, lawyers are under a professional obligation to disclose to the court any authority directly adverse to the client's position that is directly on point. This is a duty to the court that overrides the duty to the client. Failure to disclose adverse authority that is directly on point is professional misconduct.

**What must be disclosed**: Authority that is binding on the court (from a superior court in the same hierarchy) and directly on point (the facts are materially analogous and the legal principle applies to the facts of the present case).

**What need not be disclosed**: Authority from other jurisdictions that is merely persuasive; authority on tangentially related points; authority that is distinguishable on material grounds.

### Techniques for Handling Adverse Authority

**Distinguish**: Show that the material facts of the adverse case differ from the present case in a way that means the ratio does not apply.

> The defendant relies on *City of Glasgow v Taylor* [1922] 1 AC 44 (HL) for the proposition that the duty is limited to damage from a hidden defect that the occupier knew was present but concealed. In that case, the dangerous condition was the poisonous berries hidden within an attractive plant. Unlike *Glasgow v Taylor*, there is no allegation in the present case that the defect was hidden; the plaintiff's case is that the defect was obvious to an observer exercising reasonable care, which takes the present case outside the principle in *Glasgow v Taylor*.

**Confine**: Argue that the adverse case establishes a narrower proposition than the opposing party claims.

> The respondent treats *Murphy v Brentwood District Council* [1991] 1 AC 398 as excluding all liability for negligent inspection. However, the ratio of *Murphy* is limited to the recovery of pure economic loss by a subsequent purchaser from a local authority that approved defective plans. The present claim involves personal injury to an occupier resulting from a structural failure that was directly caused by the defect that was negligently approved. Personal injury claims were expressly preserved in *Murphy*: see [1991] 1 AC 398, 475 (Lord Keith).

**Criticize (where the adverse authority is not binding)**: Where the adverse authority is from a lower or equal court, argue respectfully that it was wrongly decided.

> The appellant respectfully submits that the approach taken by the court in *Jones v Brown* [2020] NSWSC 555 is incorrect and should not be followed. The court in *Jones v Brown* held that [proposition]. This reasoning is inconsistent with the High Court's subsequent analysis in *Smith v Acme Corp* (2022) 401 CLR 1, which rejected [proposition] for the reasons stated at [100]--[105]. This court should apply *Smith v Acme Corp* rather than *Jones v Brown*.

## Submissions by Court Level

### Magistrates' Court / Local Court

- Simpler matters; more informal procedure
- Written submissions may be oral only, with a brief written outline (if required by the court)
- Language less formal; less reliance on case authority
- Typical length: 5--15 pages (if written submissions are filed)

### District / County Court

- Moderate complexity; written submissions expected for contested hearings
- Both law and fact in issue at trial
- Typical length: 10--25 pages

### Supreme Court / Superior Court (Trial Division)

- Complex matters; formal procedure
- Detailed written submissions expected; strict compliance with practice directions
- Affidavit evidence central to interlocutory applications
- Typical length: 15--40 pages

### Court of Appeal / Intermediate Appellate Courts

- Legal issues only (no re-determination of facts unless error of fact alleged)
- Must identify the specific error in the decision below
- Must address the standard of appellate review (*House v The King* (1936) 55 CLR 499 (HCA) for discretionary decisions: error of law, acting on a wrong principle, allowing extraneous or irrelevant matters to guide the decision, mistaking the facts, or failing to take into account a material consideration)
- Typical length: 20--50 pages

### Highest Courts (High Court of Australia, US Supreme Court, UK Supreme Court, SCC, NZSC)

- Only matters of genuine public importance or significant legal principle
- Submissions must establish why the case warrants the court's attention (special leave / certiorari applications)
- Strict word limits and formatting requirements
- Amicus curiae (friend of the court) submissions are common at this level
- Typical length: 20--50 pages (varies by court rules and matter complexity)

## Oral Submissions

Written submissions are typically supplemented by oral argument. The oral submission should complement, not simply repeat, the written submission.

### Preparation

1. **Know the submission deeply**: The judge will ask questions about any part of it. You must be able to answer without referring to the document.
2. **Identify the two or three key propositions**: These are the points you must drive home.
3. **Anticipate questions**: What will the court ask about your weakest arguments? About the adverse authorities? About the unusual facts? Prepare answers.
4. **Mark the authorities**: Flag the specific passages in the case reports you intend to take the court to. Do not read whole judgments; take the court to the specific paragraphs.

### Structure of Oral Argument

1. **Opening**: State who you represent and the relief you seek. Then state the case in one or two sentences: "This is an appeal from a finding of negligence against a property valuer. The appellant submits that the primary judge erred in finding that the disclaimer clause did not negate the duty of care."
2. **Roadmap**: Tell the court the structure of the argument: "I will address three points. First, the effect of the disclaimer. Second, the applicable standard of care. Third, if your Honour disagrees on those points, the quantum of damages."
3. **Address the issues**: Follow the roadmap. Use the authority passages you have prepared. Be responsive to questions --- a question from the bench is an opportunity to demonstrate your command of the material.
4. **Respond to the opponent's argument**: After the opponent has addressed the court, respond to their key points succinctly.
5. **Conclusion**: Restate the key proposition and the orders sought.
