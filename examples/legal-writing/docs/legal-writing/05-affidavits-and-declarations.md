# Affidavits and Declarations

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-05
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf`](crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf), [`uksc-practice-directions.pdf`](uksc-practice-directions.pdf), [`precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf`](precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf)

An affidavit is a written statement of fact, sworn or affirmed under oath before an authorized witness, that serves as evidence in legal proceedings. It is the primary means by which parties introduce evidence in interlocutory applications and plays a significant role in final hearings across many jurisdictions, particularly in equity, administrative law, and commercial proceedings where the evidence turns on documentary records rather than contested oral testimony.

The rules governing affidavits are strict because affidavit evidence bypasses the ordinary safeguards of oral testimony: cross-examination, the court's direct observation of witness demeanor, and the opportunity for real-time challenges. Failure to comply with affidavit rules can result in the evidence being rejected, portions being struck out, or the deponent being exposed to perjury or contempt proceedings.

This document covers affidavit structure and drafting, rules governing admissible content, hearsay and opinion evidence, exhibit procedures, execution requirements across jurisdictions, common defects, and statutory declarations as a complementary instrument.

## Affidavit Structure

An affidavit has a defined formal structure. Departing from it without good reason is likely to result in an objection to admissibility or a direction from the court to re-file.

### 1. Title Block

The affidavit opens with the title of proceedings, identifying the court, the parties, and the proceeding number. This block is essential for filing and cross-referencing.

```
IN THE FEDERAL COURT OF AUSTRALIA
NEW SOUTH WALES DISTRICT REGISTRY
GENERAL DIVISION

Proceeding No: NSD 1234 of 2026

BETWEEN:    JANE LI CHEN
            Plaintiff

AND:        ACME VALUATIONS PTY LTD
            Defendant

AFFIDAVIT OF JANE LI CHEN
SWORN ON [date]
```

### 2. Deponent Identification

The opening paragraph identifies the person making the affidavit (the "deponent") with sufficient detail to establish their identity and competence.

Standard format:
> I, Jane Li Chen, of 45 Harbour Road, Sydney, in the State of New South Wales, Business Executive, say on oath [or: make oath and say] [or: do solemnly and sincerely affirm]:

Include:
- Full legal name
- Residential or business address (not merely the suburb)
- Occupation
- The form of the oath or affirmation (the deponent's choice; there is no legal distinction in evidential weight between an oath and an affirmation)

### 3. Body: Numbered Paragraphs

The body of the affidavit consists of consecutively numbered paragraphs. Each paragraph contains one statement of fact or group of closely related facts.

**Paragraph structure principles**:

- **One fact (or closely related group) per paragraph**: This facilitates cross-referencing in submissions and targeted challenge in cross-examination. Paragraph 12 can be referred to in cross-examination without the deponent needing to search through a long paragraph.
- **State the source of knowledge**: For each fact, the deponent must state how they know it. Direct observation, personal action, personal receipt of a document, or information from a named third party.
- **Simple, direct language**: The affidavit is a formal document but not a legal treatise. Use the first person and present facts as the deponent perceived them.
- **Chronological order** (where possible): Chronological organization makes the narrative easiest to follow and facilitates cross-examination.
- **Reference exhibits by their code**: "A copy of the valuation report is exhibited to this affidavit and marked 'JLC-1.'"

**Example paragraphs**:

> 1. I am the plaintiff in these proceedings.
>
> 2. On 10 January 2025, I engaged the defendant to provide a valuation of the property at 123 Main Street, Springfield, New South Wales ("the Property"). A copy of my engagement letter is exhibited to this affidavit and marked "JLC-1."
>
> 3. In my engagement letter, I stated that I intended to rely on the valuation to decide whether to purchase the Property.
>
> 4. On 15 January 2025, I received a written valuation report from the defendant in the amount of $1,200,000. A copy of the valuation report is exhibited to this affidavit and marked "JLC-2."
>
> 5. On 28 February 2025, in reliance on the valuation in JLC-2, I purchased the Property for $1,150,000. A copy of the Contract for Sale is exhibited to this affidavit and marked "JLC-3."
>
> 6. On 15 June 2025, I commissioned an independent valuation of the Property from Mr. David Park, a registered valuer. A copy of Mr. Park's report is exhibited to this affidavit and marked "JLC-4." Mr. Park valued the Property at $850,000 as at 15 January 2025.

Note: Paragraph 6 refers to Mr. Park's report but does not give his opinion as if it were the deponent's own knowledge. The exhibit attaches Mr. Park's report; his evidence must be given in his own affidavit.

### 4. Jurat

The jurat is the concluding block of the affidavit. It records:
- The oath or affirmation formula
- The date of swearing
- The place of swearing
- The signature of the deponent
- The signature and qualification of the authorized witness

```
Sworn [or Affirmed] at Sydney
in the State of New South Wales
on [date]

Before me:

______________________________
[Full name of witness]
[Qualification, e.g.: Justice of the Peace / Solicitor of the Supreme Court of NSW / Notary Public]
[Address or registration number where required]
```

The jurat must be completed and signed in the presence of the deponent at the time the affidavit is sworn or affirmed. A jurat that is signed at a different time, or by a witness who was not present, renders the affidavit defective.

## Rules About Admissible Content

### Direct Knowledge: The Fundamental Rule

The fundamental rule of affidavit evidence is that the deponent must confine their evidence to facts within their own direct knowledge. The deponent must state how they know each fact.

**Forms of direct knowledge**:
- **Personal observation**: "I saw the defendant enter the premises at approximately 2:15 pm."
- **Personal action**: "I signed the contract on 10 February 2025."
- **Personal receipt**: "I received the email marked 'JLC-5' on 12 February 2025."
- **Personal statement**: "I said to the defendant, 'I intend to rely entirely on your valuation.'"

The deponent cannot state, as personal knowledge, facts that they did not personally observe or participate in. "The defendant's property inspection was inadequate" is not a fact within the deponent's personal knowledge (unless the deponent was present at the inspection). It is also an opinion, which is separately inadmissible (see below).

### Hearsay Evidence in Affidavits

Hearsay is a statement made by a person other than the deponent, tendered to prove the truth of the contents of that statement. The general rule is that hearsay is inadmissible. However, the exceptions in affidavit practice are significant:

**1. Interlocutory proceedings**

In interlocutory applications (injunctions, summary judgment, security for costs, discovery applications), hearsay evidence is generally admissible, because the urgency and provisional nature of the proceeding is inconsistent with requiring all evidence to be sworn from direct knowledge. However, the court will typically give hearsay evidence less weight than direct evidence, and the source of the hearsay must be identified.

Rule: *Federal Court Rules 2011* (Cth) r 5.02; *Civil Procedure Rules 1998* (UK) PD 32 para 18.2.

Required formula when using hearsay in interlocutory affidavits:
> 7. I am informed by [full name and relationship to the proceedings] and I verily believe that [statement of the hearsay fact].

**2. Business records exceptions**

Records made in the ordinary course of business may be admitted as proof of the facts recorded, under the business records exceptions in most evidence statutes:
- Australia (federal): *Evidence Act 1995* (Cth) ss 69--70
- United States: Federal Rules of Evidence, r 803(6) (records of regularly conducted activity)
- United Kingdom: *Civil Evidence Act 1995* (UK) ss 1, 9
- Canada: *Canada Evidence Act* (RSC 1985, c C-5) s 30
- New Zealand: *Evidence Act 2006* (NZ) s 19

**3. Admissions**

A statement by a party against interest (an admission) is admissible as evidence of the fact admitted, both at common law and under most evidence legislation.

### Facts vs Opinions: The Inadmissibility of Lay Opinion

An affidavit must contain facts, not the deponent's opinions about what the facts mean or what conclusions should be drawn from them.

**Inadmissible (lay opinion)**:
> "The defendant was negligent in preparing the valuation." (This is a legal conclusion, not a fact.)
> "The defendant clearly did not carry out proper due diligence." (This is a characterization, not a fact.)
> "The property was obviously worth much less than the defendant's valuation." (This is an opinion.)

**Admissible (statement of fact)**:
> "The defendant's valuation report stated that the defendant had inspected the Property. The Property has a basement that was not mentioned in the valuation report." (This is a fact about what the report said and what the property contains.)

The admissibility line is between what the deponent perceived (admissible as fact) and what the deponent concludes from what they perceived (inadmissible as lay opinion). The court's job is to draw conclusions; the deponent's job is to state facts.

### Expert Opinion Evidence

Expert witnesses may include opinion evidence in their affidavits (or expert reports filed with affidavits), provided they satisfy the requirements for expert evidence:

1. The witness must have appropriate expertise in the relevant field (through training, study, or experience)
2. The opinion must fall within the scope of that expertise
3. The expert must comply with their duty to the court (all common-law jurisdictions have enshrined the principle that an expert's duty is to the court, not to the party retaining them)
4. The expert must identify the facts and assumptions on which the opinion is based

**Expert evidence rules by jurisdiction:**

| Jurisdiction | Key Rules | Key Cases |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| Australia (Federal Court) | FCR 2011 Pt 23; Expert Evidence Practice Note (GPN-EXPT) | *Makita (Australia) Pty Ltd v Sprowles* (2001) 52 NSWLR 705 (NSWCA) |
| Australia (Evidence Act jurisdictions) | *Evidence Act 1995* (Cth) s 79 (opinion evidence exception) | *Dasreef Pty Ltd v Hawchar* (2011) 243 CLR 588 (HCA) |
| United States | FRE r 702 | *Daubert v Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.* 509 US 579 (1993); *General Electric Co v Joiner* 522 US 136 (1997) |
| United Kingdom | CPR Part 35; Practice Direction 35 | *ICI Chemicals & Polymers Ltd v TTE Training Ltd* [2007] EWCA Civ 725 |
| Canada | Common law: expertise, necessity, reliability, absence of exclusionary rule | *R v Mohan* [1994] 2 SCR 9 (SCC); *R v J-LJ* 2000 SCC 51 |
| New Zealand | *Evidence Act 2006* (NZ) ss 25--26 | *Mahomed v R* [2011] NZCA 404 |

## Exhibit Procedure

### Exhibits vs Annexures

**Exhibits**: Documents that are separately attached to the affidavit and identified by an exhibit certificate. Each exhibit is a separate document (or bundle of documents) with its own exhibit certificate signed by the authorized witness.

**Annexures**: Documents that are physically incorporated into the body of the affidavit itself. Less common in practice; used in some jurisdictions for specific purposes. The terms are sometimes used interchangeably in practice, though the technical distinction matters.

### Exhibit Identification and Numbering

Exhibits are identified by a code comprising the deponent's initials followed by a sequential number:
- Jane Li Chen, first exhibit: "JLC-1"
- John Robert Smith, second exhibit: "JRS-2"

If multiple deponents are involved in the same proceeding, each deponent's exhibits bear their own initials to avoid confusion.

### Exhibit Certificates

Each exhibit must have an exhibit certificate signed by the person before whom the affidavit was sworn:

```
This is the exhibit marked "JLC-1" referred to in the affidavit
of Jane Li Chen sworn before me on [date].

______________________________
[Witness signature]
[Name of witness]
[Qualification]
```

The certificate is placed on the face of the exhibit (or as a cover sheet if the exhibit is a multi-page document). The certificate must be signed at the same time as the affidavit is sworn --- it cannot be signed retrospectively.

### Document Bundles

Multiple related documents may be exhibited as a single bundle:
> "Exhibited to this affidavit and marked 'JLC-5' is a bundle of emails exchanged between me and the defendant between 1 January 2025 and 28 February 2025. Within 'JLC-5', the emails are arranged in chronological order."

For very large exhibits, check whether the court's practice direction permits electronic filing or requires a paginated paper bundle. Many modern courts have specific rules about the format and size of exhibit bundles filed electronically.

### Common Exhibit Errors

| Error | Consequence | Prevention |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Exhibit certificate not signed by the witness | The exhibit may be inadmissible or objected to | Check every exhibit certificate before filing |
| Exhibit referenced in the body but not attached | The reference is meaningless; the court has no exhibit to read | Use a checklist; cross-check every "JLC-X" reference |
| Exhibit number does not match the body reference | Confusion; may require a supplementary affidavit to correct | Maintain a numbered list of exhibits as you draft |
| Illegible copies | Court cannot read the evidence | Use high-quality copies; use originals for critical documents |
| Undated documents | Reduced evidentiary weight; timeline cannot be established | Date-stamp documents when received |
| Unauthorized redaction | Redacted material may be the most probative | Do not redact unless legally justified (privilege, confidentiality order) |

## Execution Requirements

### By Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Authorized Witnesses | Source |
|-------------|---------------------|--------|
| Australia (Federal Court) | Justice of the Peace, solicitor of the Supreme Court, notary public, authorized officer | *Federal Court Rules 2011* (Cth) Pt 5; *Oaths Act 1900* (NSW) (for NSW oaths) |
| Australia (NSW) | Any of the authorized witnesses under the *Oaths Act 1900* (NSW) | *Oaths Act 1900* (NSW) ss 26--27 |
| Australia (Vic) | Justice of the Peace, solicitor, notary public, police officer | *Oaths and Affirmations Act 2018* (Vic) |
| United States (federal) | Notary public (in all states); some states permit additional authorized persons | Fed. R. Civ. P. r 56(c)(4) (for summary judgment); state law otherwise |
| United Kingdom | Solicitor, commissioner for oaths, authorized court officer | CPR PD 32 |
| Canada (federal) | Commissioner for taking affidavits, notary public, solicitor | *Canada Evidence Act* (RSC 1985, c C-5) s 53; provincial law varies |
| New Zealand | Solicitor, Justice of the Peace, registrar or deputy registrar of a court | *Oaths and Declarations Act 1957* (NZ) |

### Remote Witnessing

Following COVID-19, most jurisdictions introduced provisions for remote witnessing of affidavits via audiovisual link. Many of these provisions have been made permanent:

- **Australia**: Various state and federal instruments; see *Electronic Transactions Act 1999* (Cth) and state equivalents; specific court practice notes
- **United Kingdom**: CPR PD 32 permits remote witnessing in specified circumstances
- **Canada**: Provincial regulations; e.g., Ontario's *Commissioner for Taking Affidavits Act*, O. Reg. 431/20 (now permanent)
- **New Zealand**: *Oaths and Declarations Amendment Act 2020* (NZ) introduced permanent remote witnessing provisions

**Practical requirements for remote witnessing**: All jurisdictions require that (1) the witness can see the deponent and observe the signing; (2) the deponent can see and hear the witness; (3) specific compliance steps are followed; and (4) the jurat reflects the remote execution. The specific requirements vary; always check current rules before proceeding.

## Common Affidavit Defects

Practitioners frequently make the following errors when drafting or supervising the preparation of affidavits:

| Defect | Description | Prevention |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Argumentative content | The affidavit argues the case rather than stating facts | Remove all legal submissions, characterizations, and conclusions; these belong in written submissions |
| Scandalous or oppressive content | Irrelevant, inflammatory, or prejudicial material | Confine the affidavit to relevant facts; delete personal attacks |
| Vague timing | "In or around early 2025" when a precise date is ascertainable | Pin down dates with documentary records; if approximate, state the basis |
| Source not identified | "I am informed that..." without saying by whom | Always identify the source: "I am informed by David Park, the independent valuer, that..." |
| Inconsistency with documentary record | The affidavit says X but the exhibited documents show Y | Cross-check the affidavit against all exhibits before filing |
| Missing exhibits | An exhibit is referenced but not attached | Use a checklist; verify before filing |
| Over-length / repetition | Paragraphs repeat information or include irrelevant detail | Edit for relevance and concision; each paragraph should add something new |
| Conclusions of law | "The contract was void for uncertainty" | State the factual basis (the specific terms that were missing or contradictory); let the court draw the legal conclusion |

## Statutory Declarations

A statutory declaration is a written statement of fact, declared (not sworn) to be true before an authorized witness. It is used in non-litigation contexts and in some regulatory and administrative settings.

### When to Use a Statutory Declaration vs an Affidavit

**Use an affidavit when**: The statement is to be used as evidence in court proceedings; the court rules require an affidavit; the statement will be subject to cross-examination.

**Use a statutory declaration when**: The statement is being made for a government agency or regulatory body that requires one by statute; the context is non-contentious (e.g., change of name, lost documents, probate applications); the relevant statute requires a statutory declaration rather than an affidavit.

### Structure of a Statutory Declaration (Australian Style)

> STATUTORY DECLARATION
>
> I, Jane Li Chen, of 45 Harbour Road, Sydney, New South Wales, Business Executive, make the following declaration under the *Statutory Declarations Act 1959* (Cth):
>
> [Numbered paragraphs of factual declarations]
>
> I declare that the matters stated in this declaration are true to the best of my knowledge and belief.
>
> Declared at Sydney on [date]
>
> Before me:
> ______________________________
> [Name and qualification of authorized witness]

### Criminal Consequences of False Declarations

Making a false statutory declaration is a criminal offense in all jurisdictions:

| Jurisdiction | Offense | Maximum Penalty |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| Australia (Commonwealth) | *Statutory Declarations Act 1959* (Cth) s 11 | 4 years imprisonment |
| Australia (NSW) | *Oaths Act 1900* (NSW) | Varies |
| United Kingdom | *Perjury Act 1911* (UK) s 5 | 2 years imprisonment |
| Canada | *Criminal Code* (RSC 1985, c C-46) s 134 | 2 years imprisonment |
| New Zealand | *Crimes Act 1961* (NZ) s 111 | 3 years imprisonment |

This criminal liability should be explained to the client or witness before they make any declaration. The explanation should be documented in the file.
