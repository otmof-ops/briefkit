# Pleadings and Court Documents

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-06
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`uksc-practice-directions.pdf`](uksc-practice-directions.pdf), [`crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf`](crown-court-compendium-part-i-2024.pdf), [`precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf`](precision-clarity-legal-writing.pdf)

Pleadings define the boundaries of a legal dispute. They state the material facts on which each party relies, identify the legal causes of action and defenses, and frame the issues that will be determined at trial. Everything that follows in litigation --- discovery, evidence, submissions, judgment --- is shaped by what is (and is not) pleaded. A party cannot rely on a fact at trial that it has not pleaded; a cause of action not raised in the pleadings cannot be pursued (subject to amendment).

This document covers the principal types of pleadings, the fundamental rule about material facts, the rules governing their content and form, amendment procedures, strike-out applications, default judgment, and the pleading requirements for specific causes of action.

---

## The Function of Pleadings

Pleadings serve four core functions:

1. **Notice**: Each party is formally informed of the case it must meet at trial. The plaintiff knows what defenses will be raised. The defendant knows what claims will be pursued. The principle of natural justice (audi alteram partem --- hear the other side) is given concrete effect through the pleadings.

2. **Issue definition**: The exchange of pleadings identifies which facts are in dispute and which are admitted. Admitted facts need not be proved at trial. The issues for determination at trial are the facts that have been denied or not admitted.

3. **Scope limitation**: The pleadings define the scope of the proceeding. A party cannot rely on a fact that it has not pleaded and cannot pursue a cause of action it has not raised (subject to the court's discretion to allow amendment). This prevents ambush and ensures procedural fairness.

4. **Case management**: Courts manage litigation more efficiently when the real issues in dispute are identified early. Modern court rules in all jurisdictions require early identification of issues; pleadings are the primary mechanism.

## The Cardinal Rule: Material Facts, Not Evidence or Law

The most important rule of pleading in every common-law jurisdiction is: **plead material facts, not evidence and not law**.

### What Are Material Facts?

Material facts are the facts that, if proved, establish the elements of the cause of action or defense. They are the "who, what, when, where" of the claim: the facts that connect the parties, the conduct, the time, and the place in a way that brings the claim within the relevant legal rule.

**Example (negligent valuation claim)**:

> **3.** On 15 January 2025, the defendant provided the plaintiff with a written valuation of the property at 123 Main Street, Springfield ("the Property") in the amount of $1,200,000 ("the Valuation").
>
> **4.** The defendant did not physically inspect the Property before providing the Valuation.
>
> **5.** At the date of the Valuation, the market value of the Property was approximately $850,000.
>
> **6.** In reliance on the Valuation, the plaintiff purchased the Property on 28 February 2025 for $1,150,000.

These paragraphs state material facts. They identify the parties (defendant provided, plaintiff purchased), the conduct (providing a valuation without inspection), the deficiency (wrong value), and the plaintiff's reliance and loss.

### Not Evidence

Evidence is the material that proves the facts. Pleadings must not recite the evidence that will be used to prove the facts:

**Wrong (pleads evidence)**:
> "The defendant admitted in an internal email to John Smith dated 20 January 2025 that it had not physically inspected the Property." (This pleads the email as evidence of the fact; the fact itself is all that is needed.)

**Correct (pleads the fact)**:
> "The defendant did not inspect the Property before providing the Valuation."

The email is evidence that will be tendered at trial to prove this fact. The fact appears in the pleading; the evidence stays out of the pleading.

### Not Law

Pleadings must not plead legal conclusions:

**Wrong (pleads law)**:
> "The defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care in negligence." (Legal conclusion.)
> "The defendant's conduct was in breach of the Australian Consumer Law." (Legal conclusion.)

**Correct**:
Plead the facts that give rise to the duty and the facts that constitute the alleged breach. The legal characterization is for the court.

**Exception --- identifying the cause of action**: Most courts permit (and some require) a brief identification of the legal basis for the claim. This is not pleading a legal conclusion in detail but merely identifying the type of claim:
> "The plaintiff claims damages for breach of contract."
> "The plaintiff's claim arises under section 18 of the Australian Consumer Law."

This type of identification is not the same as pleading the legal elements of the cause of action. It is a signpost, not a pleading.

### The Authority on the Cardinal Rule

The cardinal rule has been authoritatively stated in many jurisdictions:

**Australia**: *Bruce v Odhams Press Ltd* [1936] 1 KB 697 (CA); the principle is embodied in the *Federal Court Rules 2011* (Cth) Pt 16 (which requires pleading "a concise statement of the material facts on which the party relies") and equivalent state rules.

**United Kingdom**: *Three Rivers District Council v Bank of England (No 3)* [2003] 2 AC 1 (HL): pleadings must state the material facts necessary to constitute the cause of action, but not the evidence to prove those facts.

**United States**: Fed. R. Civ. P. r 8(a): "a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief." The US "notice pleading" standard is less demanding than the "material facts" standard in Australia, UK, Canada, and NZ, which require greater specificity.

## Types of Pleadings

### Statement of Claim (or Complaint in the US)

The statement of claim is the plaintiff's pleading that initiates the proceedings. It contains:

1. **The parties**: Names, addresses (or registered offices for corporations), and descriptions in their capacities as plaintiff and defendant
2. **Material facts**: In numbered paragraphs, organized chronologically
3. **Cause(s) of action**: The legal basis for each claim (negligence, breach of contract, statutory contravention)
4. **Relief sought**: What the plaintiff asks the court to order (damages, injunction, declaration, specific performance, account of profits)

**Jurisdiction-specific terminology and rules:**

| Jurisdiction | Document Name | Governing Rule |
|-------------|--------------|----------------|
| Australia (Federal Court) | Statement of Claim | *Federal Court Rules 2011* (Cth) Pt 16 |
| Australia (NSW Supreme Court) | Statement of Claim | *Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 2005* (NSW) Pt 14 |
| Australia (NSW District Court) | Statement of Claim | *UCPR 2005* (NSW) Pt 14 |
| United States (Federal) | Complaint | Fed. R. Civ. P. r 8(a) |
| United Kingdom | Particulars of Claim | *Civil Procedure Rules 1998* (UK) Pt 16 |
| Canada (Ontario) | Statement of Claim | *Rules of Civil Procedure*, RRO 1990, Reg 194, r 25 |
| New Zealand | Statement of Claim | *High Court Rules 2016* (NZ) r 5.7 |

**US distinction**: In the United States, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure require only a "short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief": r 8(a)(2). This "notice pleading" standard is significantly less demanding than the "material facts" standard in Australia, the UK, Canada, and New Zealand. Post-*Twombly/Iqbal*, US federal courts require pleadings to be "plausible" (not merely conceivable): *Bell Atlantic Corp v Twombly* 550 US 544 (2007); *Ashcroft v Iqbal* 556 US 662 (2009).

### Defence (or Answer in the US)

The defence is the defendant's pleading in response to the statement of claim. For each paragraph of the statement of claim, the defence must state:

- **Admitted**: The defendant accepts the allegation as true. The fact need not be proved at trial.
- **Denied**: The defendant disputes the allegation. The plaintiff must prove it at trial.
- **Not admitted**: The defendant does not admit the allegation but does not positively deny it. The plaintiff must prove it. (This is appropriate where the defendant does not know whether the allegation is true.)

**Deemed admissions**: In most jurisdictions, failure to plead to a specific paragraph is treated as an admission of the fact alleged in that paragraph. Defendants must respond to every paragraph.

**Affirmative defences**: The defence must also plead any positive defences --- facts that, even if the plaintiff's allegations are proved, provide a complete or partial defence:
- Limitation: the claim is time-barred
- Contributory negligence: the plaintiff's own negligence caused or contributed to the loss
- Consent (volenti non fit injuria)
- Estoppel
- Statutory defences
- Set-off or counterclaim

An affirmative defence that is not pleaded cannot be raised at trial (subject to amendment).

### Counterclaim

A counterclaim is a claim by the defendant against the plaintiff. It is filed together with the defence and operates as a separate cause of action. The counterclaim must satisfy the same pleading requirements as a statement of claim.

**Example**: In a breach of contract claim where the plaintiff alleges the defendant failed to deliver goods, the defendant may counterclaim that the plaintiff repudiated the contract by refusing to accept delivery, and seek damages for the repudiation.

### Reply

A reply is filed by the plaintiff in response to the defence, but only if the defence raises new matters (typically affirmative defences) that the plaintiff needs to respond to. A reply is not required simply to deny the defendant's denials. If no reply is filed, the plaintiff is taken to deny all the defences pleaded.

### Cross-Claims and Third-Party Claims

**Cross-claim**: A claim by one defendant against another defendant, or by the defendant against a person not yet a party, who the defendant alleges is liable to contribute to or indemnify against the plaintiff's claim.

**Third-party notice / contribution notice**: A formal notice served by the defendant on a non-party to join them to the proceedings to pursue a contribution or indemnity claim.

## Particulars and Further Particulars

### What Are Particulars?

Particulars are details that flesh out the material facts beyond their bare statement in the pleading. They give the opposing party more specific information about the case it must meet.

**Common requirements for particulars:**

- **Negligence**: What specific acts or omissions are alleged to constitute the breach of duty? The statement of claim typically identifies the duty and its breach; the particulars of negligence specify exactly what the defendant did or failed to do.
- **Breach of contract**: Which clause was breached? On what date? What was the specific conduct constituting the breach?
- **Damages**: What are the specific heads of loss? What is the claimed quantum for each head?
- **Fraud or dishonesty**: Each specific allegation of fraud must be particularized (see below).

**Form of particulars**: Particulars may be included directly in the pleading (under a sub-heading: "Particulars of Negligence") or provided separately in response to a formal request.

### Fraud Must Be Pleaded with Particularity

In all common-law jurisdictions, allegations of fraud, dishonesty, unconscionable conduct, or other serious wrongdoing must be pleaded with specific particularity. A general allegation of fraud will be struck out.

**Why**: The requirement for particularity in fraud cases reflects the seriousness of the allegation and its potential to cause reputational damage: *Banque Commerciale SA (En Liquidation) v Akhil Holdings Ltd* (1990) 169 CLR 279, 286 (HCA); *Three Rivers District Council v Bank of England (No 3)* [2003] 2 AC 1 (HL).

**What particularity requires in fraud pleadings:**
- Identify the specific representation(s) alleged to be fraudulent
- State when and how each representation was made
- State the falsity of each representation
- State that the representation was made knowingly or recklessly (not merely negligently, which is negligent misstatement, not fraud)
- State the plaintiff's reliance on each representation
- State the loss flowing from each representation

## Amendment of Pleadings

### When Amendment Is Needed

Pleadings are frequently amended:
- Drafting defects discovered after filing (missing material facts, ambiguous allegations)
- New facts emerging from discovery or witness interviews
- New legal theories identified after the initial filing
- Withdrawal of an allegation that cannot be proved
- Addition of a new party

### Amendment Procedure: The Stage Matters

| Stage of Proceedings | Procedure |
|---------------------|-----------|
| Before the defendant files a defence | As of right (no leave required): *UCPR 2005* (NSW) r 19.1; FCR 2011 r 16.51; FRCP r 15(a) |
| After defence filed, before close of pleadings | Consent of all parties, or leave of the court |
| After close of pleadings, before trial | Leave of the court required; increasing scrutiny as trial approaches |
| At trial | Leave may be granted if no prejudice to the opposing party that cannot be compensated by costs |
| After judgment | Generally not permitted except to cure a formal defect |

### The Court's Discretion to Grant Leave to Amend

When leave is required, the court considers:

1. **Prejudice**: Would the amendment cause the opposing party a prejudice that cannot be adequately compensated by costs? This is the dominant consideration.
2. **Timing**: How late in the proceedings is the amendment sought? Late amendments at or after the commencement of trial are subject to more scrutiny.
3. **Explanation**: Has the applicant explained why the amendment was not made earlier? An unexplained late amendment attracts less sympathy.
4. **Merits**: Does the proposed amendment disclose an arguable case?

**Leading authority on amendment (Australia)**: *Aon Risk Services Australia Ltd v Australian National University* (2009) 239 CLR 175 (HCA): the High Court held that case management considerations (the impact of late amendments on the conduct of proceedings and on other court users) are directly relevant to the exercise of the discretion to grant leave to amend. The "interests of justice" encompass interests of other litigants and the justice system as a whole, not just the interests of the parties to the immediate litigation.

## Strike-Out Applications

A party may apply to strike out (or have dismissed) all or part of the opposing party's pleading.

**Grounds for strike-out:**

| Ground | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| No reasonable cause of action / defence | Even if all facts pleaded are proved, no recognized legal claim is made out | A claim for "distress" not connected to any recognized cause of action |
| Embarrassing / insufficient | The pleading is so vague or ambiguous that the opposing party cannot understand the case it must meet | Pleading "the defendant acted unlawfully" without specifying any act or legal rule |
| Scandalous | Irrelevant, inflammatory, or prejudicial material | Personal attacks on the opposing party's character with no legal relevance |
| Frivolous or vexatious | The pleading has no reasonable prospect of success | Relitigation of claims already finally determined (*res judicata*) |
| Abuse of process | The proceeding is an improper use of the court's process | Bringing proceedings for an improper collateral purpose |
| Failure to plead with particularity | Fraud or serious wrongdoing alleged without the requisite particulars | A fraud pleading that fails to identify the specific misrepresentations |

Strike-out is a serious remedy that the court exercises with care. Courts generally prefer to allow amendment rather than strike out if the defect can be cured. A pleading is not struck out merely because it is poorly drafted; it must disclose a fundamental defect.

## Default Judgment

If the defendant fails to file a defence within the time prescribed by the court rules (or a time extended by order), the plaintiff may apply for default judgment. Default judgment is judgment in the plaintiff's favor without a trial.

**Requirements for default judgment:**
1. The proceedings must have been properly served on the defendant
2. The time for filing the defence must have expired
3. No defence has been filed
4. For claims for unliquidated damages, the court may require an assessment of damages

**Setting aside default judgment**: A defendant who has had default judgment entered against them may apply to set it aside. The court will consider:
- Does the defendant have an arguable defence?
- Is there a satisfactory explanation for the failure to file a defence?
- Would the plaintiff be prejudiced by setting aside the judgment in a way that cannot be compensated by costs?

## Pleading Specific Causes of Action

### Contract

**Plead:**
1. The existence of the contract: date, parties, whether written or oral, and (if written) identification of the document
2. The relevant terms: express terms by quotation or reference; implied terms by identifying the basis of implication (term implied in fact, by law, by custom)
3. Performance by the plaintiff of all conditions precedent, or excuse for non-performance
4. The specific breach by the defendant: which term was breached; what the defendant did or failed to do; when
5. Loss and damage: each item of loss with particulars of quantum

**Common pitfalls**: Failing to plead that the plaintiff performed its own obligations (the defendant will plead this as a defence if omitted). Failing to plead the existence of a condition precedent that must be satisfied before the defendant's obligation arises.

### Negligence

**Plead:**
1. The facts giving rise to a duty of care: the relationship between the parties, the foreseeability of harm, the vulnerability of the plaintiff (as relevant)
2. The standard of care: what a reasonable person in the defendant's position would have done
3. The specific breach: what the defendant did or failed to do that fell below the standard
4. Causation (factual causation / "but for"): how the breach caused the loss
5. Remoteness: that the type of loss was a reasonably foreseeable consequence
6. Loss and damage: each item of loss

### Statutory Causes of Action

**Plead:**
1. The specific provision of the statute alleged to have been contravened
2. The facts that bring the defendant's conduct within the provision
3. The relief available under the statute and the plaintiff's entitlement to it
4. Where the statute provides a right of action for a private plaintiff, the basis for standing

**Australian Consumer Law**: When pleading a contravention of the *Australian Consumer Law* (*Competition and Consumer Act 2010* (Cth) Sch 2):
- Identify the specific provision (e.g., s 18: misleading or deceptive conduct; s 29: false representations)
- Plead the specific conduct alleged to contravene the provision
- Plead that the conduct was "in trade or commerce" (if required by the provision)
- Plead the loss or damage suffered and its connection to the contravention

## Court Documents: Format Requirements

### Common Formatting Requirements

Most jurisdictions have specific formatting requirements for court documents. Check the court's practice directions before filing. Common requirements:

| Element | Typical Requirement |
|---------|---------------------|
| Paper size | A4 (AU, UK, NZ, CA) or Letter (US) |
| Font | Times New Roman or Arial, 12 point (AU; varies) |
| Line spacing | 1.5 or double (varies by court) |
| Margins | 25mm all sides (typical) |
| Paragraph numbering | Sequential numbering required by most rules |
| Cover sheet | Required by most superior courts |
| Filing party details | Firm name, solicitor, address for service, file reference |

### Filing Procedures

**Electronic filing**: Most superior courts in all six jurisdictions now require or accept electronic filing. Check:
- Which documents must be filed electronically
- Accepted file formats (PDF is universal; some courts accept Word)
- File size limits
- Portal registration requirements

**Service**: After filing, most documents must be served on all other parties. Service requirements (method, time, form of confirmation) vary by jurisdiction and document type. For initiating documents (statements of claim), personal service is generally required. For subsequent documents, service by email is usually permitted.
