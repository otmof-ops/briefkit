# Legislative Drafting

**Section Code:** AF-PRAC-02-07
**Document Type:** Technical Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-30

## Overview

**Paper section references:** [`essentials-drafting-clear-legal-rules.pdf`](essentials-drafting-clear-legal-rules.pdf), [`nzlc-report-35-legislation-manual.pdf`](nzlc-report-35-legislation-manual.pdf), [`oecd-checklist-law-drafting-regulatory-management.pdf`](oecd-checklist-law-drafting-regulatory-management.pdf), [`quick-guide-legislative-drafting.pdf`](quick-guide-legislative-drafting.pdf)

Legislative drafting is the process of translating policy decisions into statutory text. It is the most consequential form of legal writing: a statute, once enacted, creates binding rules that apply to every person within the jurisdiction, are enforced by state power, and may remain in force for generations. The drafter must produce text that is precise, internally consistent, comprehensive in its coverage of intended situations, and capable of being applied to situations the drafter cannot fully anticipate.

This document covers the structure of bills and statutes, delegated legislation, drafting conventions (including the "shall"/"must" distinction, provisos, and definitions), commencement and transitional provisions, sunset clauses, and the role of parliamentary drafting offices.

## Bill Structure

A bill is a proposed statute introduced into the legislature. The structure of a bill follows established conventions that are broadly consistent across common-law jurisdictions, though the specific terminology and formatting vary.

### Standard Components

#### 1. Long Title (or Purpose Clause)

The long title states the subject matter and purpose of the bill. It is part of the enacted Act and may be used as an aid to statutory interpretation.

**Modern style (Australia)**:
> A Bill for an Act to regulate the collection, use, and disclosure of personal information, and for related purposes.

**Older style (Victorian-era UK legislation)**:
> An Act for consolidating with Amendments the Law relating to the Sale of Land and other Property of Deceased Persons and the Payment of their Debts, and for other purposes connected with the Administration of Estates.

Modern drafting offices favor concise long titles supplemented by express objects clauses within the body of the Act.

#### 2. Enacting Formula

The enacting formula is the formal words that give the legislation constitutional authority. It is a constitutional requirement in all parliamentary systems and forms part of the Act.

**Australia (Commonwealth)**:
> The Parliament of Australia enacts:

**United Kingdom**:
> Be it enacted by the King's most Excellent Majesty, by and with the advice and consent of the Lords Spiritual and Temporal, and Commons, in this present Parliament assembled, and by the authority of the same, as follows:

**Canada**:
> Her Majesty [or His Majesty], by and with the advice and consent of the Senate and House of Commons of Canada, enacts as follows:

**New Zealand**:
> The Parliament of New Zealand enacts as follows:

**United States**:
> Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the United States of America in Congress assembled,...

#### 3. Preliminary Provisions

Preliminary provisions include:
- **Name of the Act**: "This Act is the Privacy Act 2026" or "This Act may be cited as the Privacy Act 2026."
- **Commencement**: When the Act (or various parts of it) comes into force. See the section on commencement below.
- **Application and scope**: The geographic scope (does it apply to conduct outside the jurisdiction?), the entities it binds (does it bind the Crown?), and any categories of person or conduct excluded.
- **Objects clause**: Where included, states the Act's purposes or objects.
- **Definitions**: General definitions applying throughout the Act (often in a separate section or schedule).

#### 4. Hierarchical Structure

Statutes are organized into a hierarchy of units:

| Level | AU / UK / NZ / CA | US |
|-------|-------------------|-----|
| Highest | Part | Title |
| Mid-level | Division (or Chapter) | Subtitle |
| Section group | Subdivision | Chapter |
| Basic unit | **Section** | **Section** |
| Sub-unit | Subsection | Subsection |
| Further sub-unit | Paragraph | Paragraph |
| Lowest | Subparagraph | Subparagraph |

**Example hierarchy (Australian style):**

```
Part 3 --- Collection of Personal Information

Division 1 --- General Principles

Section 15 --- Collection must be lawful and fair
    (1) An entity must not collect personal information unless the
        collection is:
        (a) reasonably necessary for one or more of the entity's
            functions or activities; and
        (b) by lawful and fair means.
    (2) An entity must collect personal information directly from
        the individual unless it is unreasonable or impracticable
        to do so.
```

#### 5. Definitions Section

Most modern statutes include a definitions section (typically numbered s 3 or s 4, or grouped in a schedule). **Drafting conventions for definitions:**

**"Means" (exhaustive definition)**: The term has only the meaning given.
> "Personal information" means information or an opinion about an identified individual, or an individual who is reasonably identifiable, whether the information or opinion is true or not and whether the information or opinion is recorded in a material form or not.

**"Includes" (expansive definition)**: The term retains its ordinary meaning and is extended to include the listed items.
> "Document" includes:
> (a) a map, plan, diagram, or other graphic;
> (b) a photograph;
> (c) a film, tape, disc, or other device in or on which information is stored.

**Cross-referenced definition**: Where a term is defined in another Act, incorporate by reference:
> "Entity" has the same meaning as in the *Privacy Act 1988*.

**Listed in alphabetical order**: Definitions should be in alphabetical order for ease of reference. Some drafting offices group definitions by subject matter for very long definitions sections, but alphabetical order is the default.

#### 6. Schedules

Schedules are appended to the Act and form part of it. They are used for:
- Detailed technical provisions that would interrupt the flow of the main text
- Forms, tables, and formulas
- Transitional, savings, and consequential amendment provisions
- Lists of controlled or regulated items
- Amendment tables (showing how other Acts are amended)

## Delegated Legislation

### What Is Delegated Legislation?

Delegated legislation is law made by a person or body authorized by an Act of Parliament (the enabling Act or parent Act). The enabling Act grants the power; the delegated legislation exercises it.

**Why delegated legislation exists:**
- **Technical complexity**: Parliament can establish the policy framework; technical details (thresholds, procedures, standards) are delegated to the executive.
- **Flexibility**: Delegated legislation can be amended more quickly than primary legislation, enabling regulatory responses to changing circumstances.
- **Urgency**: In emergencies, the executive may need to act faster than the parliamentary process allows.
- **Volume**: The sheer quantity of regulatory detail that modern government requires cannot be enacted as primary legislation.

### Types

| Type | Description | Who Makes It |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Regulations | The most common form; detailed rules implementing the parent Act | Governor-General (AU Commonwealth); Governor in Council (AU states, CA); King in Council / Secretary of State (UK); Minister |
| Rules | Court procedure, professional standards, and similar operational matters | Rule committees; Ministers |
| Orders | Executive instruments for specific applications of a general power | Governor-General; Secretary of State; Minister |
| By-laws | Local government or statutory body legislation | Local councils; statutory corporations |
| Statutory Instruments (UK) | The UK umbrella term for most delegated legislation | Usually the responsible Secretary of State |

### Ultra Vires: The Fundamental Limit

Any provision of delegated legislation that exceeds the scope of the power conferred by the enabling Act is *ultra vires* (beyond power) and is void. Courts have struck down delegated legislation on this basis.

**The scope question**: When the enabling Act says "the Minister may make regulations for the purposes of this Act," the question is: does a particular regulation further the purposes of the Act, or does it go beyond those purposes? Courts interpret the enabling power generously but not without limit.

**Key cases on ultra vires delegated legislation:**
- *Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority* (1998) 194 CLR 355 (HCA): established the approach to determining whether breach of a condition in delegated legislation renders conduct invalid or merely unlawful
- *R (Alconbury Developments Ltd) v Secretary of State* [2003] 2 AC 295 (HL): ultra vires principles in UK context

## Drafting Conventions

### "Shall" vs "Must"

The distinction between "shall" and "must" has been one of the most debated questions in legislative drafting for decades.

**Traditional use of "shall"**: In Victorian-era legislation (and persisting into the late twentieth century), "shall" was used to:
- Impose mandatory obligations: "The trustee shall maintain accounts."
- Create future contingencies: "If the application is approved, the applicant shall be entitled to..."
- Confer rights (as "shall be entitled to"): "The employee shall be entitled to four weeks' annual leave."

**The problem with "shall"**: "Shall" is grammatically and semantically ambiguous. It can mean:
- A mandatory obligation (you are required to do X)
- A prediction (this will happen)
- A contingent outcome (if A, then B will follow)
- A right (you are entitled to X)

This ambiguity produces interpretation disputes. In *Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority* (1998) 194 CLR 355, [93], the High Court observed that the use of "shall" versus "must" does not reliably indicate whether a provision is mandatory or directory.

**Modern position by jurisdiction:**

| Jurisdiction | Position on "Shall" | Preferred Term for Obligations |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| Australia (Commonwealth) | "Shall" abandoned in modern Commonwealth legislation; use "must" for obligations | "Must" |
| United Kingdom | OPC Drafting Guidance (2020) recommends "must" for obligations | "Must" |
| Canada | Department of Justice guide recommends "must" for obligations | "Must" |
| New Zealand | PCO style guide requires "must" for obligations | "Must" |
| United States (federal) | "Shall" remains common in federal legislation; proposals to standardize have not been adopted | "Shall" (in practice); "must" (in some modern statutes) |

**Current Australian practice**: The Australian Commonwealth drafting office uses:
- "Must" for mandatory obligations (the person is required to do X)
- "Must not" for prohibitions
- "May" for discretionary powers
- "May not" (rare) for discretionary exclusions
- "Is entitled to" for rights
- "Is not required to" for negation of obligations

### "May" vs "Must"

This distinction is critical:
- **"Must"**: The person or body is required to act. Failure to act is unlawful.
- **"May"**: The person or body has a discretion; they are permitted to act but are not required to.

This distinction can be outcome-determinative in litigation. A court reviewing a statutory decision will ask whether the decision-maker had a duty to act (must) or a discretion (may).

**Example**:
> The Minister **must** publish the annual report within 60 days after the end of each financial year. (Mandatory; breach is unlawful.)
>
> The Minister **may** extend the publication deadline by up to 14 days if the Minister considers this necessary. (Discretionary; the Minister has a choice but may only exercise it for the specified purpose.)

### Provisos and Exceptions

**The traditional proviso**: Older legislation frequently used the structure "X: Provided that Y" to introduce exceptions or conditions. This structure is now deprecated in all major drafting offices for the same reason "shall" is deprecated: it is ambiguous about scope.

**The ambiguity of provisos**: A proviso ("Provided that...") may qualify only the immediately preceding provision, or it may qualify a broader range of provisions. Courts have spent considerable time determining the scope of provisos. Modern drafting eliminates this ambiguity by using explicit structure.

**Modern alternative to provisos**:

*Old style*:
> A person must not disclose personal information to a third party: Provided that this prohibition does not apply where the disclosure is required by law.

*Modern style*:
> (1) A person must not disclose personal information to a third party.
> (2) Subsection (1) does not apply if the disclosure is required or authorised by or under a law of the Commonwealth or a State.

The modern style makes the relationship between the general rule (subsection (1)) and the exception (subsection (2)) immediately clear.

### Definitions: The "Means/Includes" Distinction in Practice

When drafting definitions, the choice between "means" and "includes" determines whether the definition is exhaustive or expansive:

| Formulation | Effect | When to Use |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| "X means Y" | X has only the meaning in Y; ordinary meaning is displaced | When precise, limited scope is required |
| "X includes Y" | X has its ordinary meaning plus the listed items in Y | When you want to expand an ordinary-meaning term |
| "X means Y, and includes Z" | X is limited to Y but extended to include Z | When you want both precision and extension |

**Practical example:**

*Too narrow (over-"means")*:
> "Animal" means a dog or a cat.

Under this definition, "animal" in the Act does not include horses, cows, or birds. If the Act was intended to protect all domestic animals, this definition is defective.

*Better*:
> "Animal" means any vertebrate animal, including a dog, cat, horse, or other mammal, or a bird, fish, or reptile.

*Or, if the drafter wants a floor with flexibility*:
> "Animal" means any vertebrate that is not a human being.

The choice depends on the policy intent.

## Commencement Provisions

### Why Commencement Matters

A statute does not necessarily take effect immediately upon royal assent (or presidential signature). The commencement provisions specify when the Act or particular provisions come into force. Getting this wrong can have serious consequences:
- A business that complied with regulations that had not yet commenced may have done so unnecessarily
- A court that applies a provision before it commences makes an error of law
- A transaction structured around anticipated legislation may fail if the commencement is delayed

### Types of Commencement

| Type | Description | Drafting Formula |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Commencement on assent | The Act (or provision) begins on the day Royal Assent is given | "This Act commences on the day this Act receives the Royal Assent." |
| Fixed date | The Act commences on a specified calendar date | "This Act commences on 1 July 2027." |
| Proclamation | The Act commences on a date fixed by the Governor-General (or equivalent) by proclamation published in the Gazette | "This Act commences on a day to be fixed by Proclamation." |
| Staged commencement | Different parts commence at different times | "Part 1 commences on the day this Act receives the Royal Assent. Part 2 commences on 1 January 2028. Part 3 commences on a day to be fixed by Proclamation." |
| Default commencement | If no proclamation is made within a specified period, the Act commences automatically | "If this Act has not commenced within 12 months of the day it receives the Royal Assent, it commences on the first day after that 12-month period." |

**Why proclamation commencement is used**: When the Act requires significant administrative preparation before it can operate effectively (e.g., regulations to be made, systems to be established, agencies to be set up), a proclamation commencement allows the executive to delay the operative date until the infrastructure is in place.

## Transitional Provisions

When new legislation replaces or amends existing legislation, transitional provisions manage the change-over period. They address:
- Whether matters in progress under the old Act continue under the old rules or the new rules
- Whether rights acquired under the old Act continue
- Whether obligations incurred under the old Act continue
- What happens to pending applications, investigations, or proceedings
- How determinations made under the old Act are treated

**Example transitional provision:**

> **Section 150 --- Transitional: existing contracts**
>
> (1) This Part applies to a contract entered into before the commencement of this Part (a "pre-existing contract").
>
> (2) A pre-existing contract is not invalidated by the commencement of this Part.
>
> (3) However, if a pre-existing contract is varied or renewed on or after the commencement of this Part, the varied or renewed contract is subject to this Part as if it had been entered into after commencement.
>
> (4) In this section:
> "commencement of this Part" means the day on which Part 3 of this Act commences.

### Savings Provisions

A savings provision preserves the continuing operation of something that would otherwise be affected by the new legislation:

> **Section 151 --- Savings: existing licences**
>
> A licence granted under Part 4 of the *Prior Act 2010* that was in force immediately before the commencement of this Part continues in force under this Part as if it had been granted under this Part.

## Sunset Clauses

A sunset clause provides for the automatic expiry or repeal of legislation after a specified period, without requiring Parliament to affirmatively repeal it.

**Uses of sunset clauses:**
- **Emergency legislation**: Powers granted in emergencies (pandemics, terrorist threats, natural disasters) that are not needed permanently
- **Extraordinary powers**: Legislation granting intrusive powers (surveillance, detention) that should not be permanent without periodic reauthorization
- **Pilot programs**: Experimental legislation that should be assessed before being made permanent
- **Periodic review mechanisms**: Building in mandatory reassessment

**Example:**

> **Section 200 --- Sunset**
>
> (1) This Part ceases to have effect at the end of 30 June 2028.
>
> (2) Subject to subsection (3), a thing done under this Part before it ceases to have effect is not affected.
>
> (3) On and after 1 July 2028, no person may be detained under this Part, regardless of when the detention was authorized.

Sunset clauses serve a democratic function: they ensure that extraordinary powers are periodically reviewed and cannot become permanent without active legislative renewal.

## Parliamentary Drafting Offices

Legislative drafting is a specialized professional function performed by dedicated government offices. Parliamentary drafters are public servants who spend their careers writing legislation. Their work is anonymous --- legislation does not attribute authorship to its drafter --- but the quality and consistency of their work shapes the entire legal order.

### Key Offices by Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Office | Principal Function |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| Australia (Commonwealth) | Office of Parliamentary Counsel (OPC) | Drafts all Commonwealth bills and legislative instruments |
| New South Wales | Parliamentary Counsel's Office (NSW) | Drafts all NSW bills |
| United Kingdom | Office of Parliamentary Counsel (OPC) | Drafts all UK government bills |
| Scotland | Office of the Scottish Parliamentary Counsel | Drafts Scottish government bills |
| Canada | Department of Justice, Legislative Services Branch | Drafts all federal legislation (bilingual; English and French) |
| New Zealand | Parliamentary Counsel Office (PCO) | Drafts all government bills and regulations |
| United States (House) | Office of the Legislative Counsel, House of Representatives | Drafts bills introduced in the House |
| United States (Senate) | Office of the Legislative Counsel, Senate | Drafts bills introduced in the Senate |

### The Legislative Drafting Process

In most parliamentary systems, the process follows these stages:

1. **Policy decision**: Cabinet or the responsible Minister decides on the policy to be legislated.
2. **Instructions to the drafter**: The responsible department prepares "instructions to Parliamentary Counsel" --- a detailed specification of what the legislation should achieve, including worked examples of the scenarios it should cover.
3. **Drafting**: The Parliamentary Counsel prepares drafts, consulting with the instructing department and (where the policy has legal complexity) with affected agencies, courts, or other stakeholders.
4. **Internal review**: The draft is reviewed within the drafting office for consistency, clarity, and compliance with drafting standards.
5. **Introduction**: The bill is introduced into Parliament with an explanatory memorandum (or explanatory notes) that explains each clause.
6. **Parliamentary scrutiny**: The bill passes through readings, committee consideration, and debate. Amendments may be proposed and accepted.
7. **Royal Assent**: The bill becomes an Act.
8. **Commencement**: The Act (or parts of it) commences on the specified date or by proclamation.

### Drafting Standards and Style Guides

Each drafting office maintains internal style guides and precedent banks that ensure consistency within the national legislative corpus:
- **Australia (OPC)**: The OPC publishes *Drafting Directions* and maintains a Legislation Handbook
- **United Kingdom**: The OPC publishes *Drafting Guidance* (updated 2020; publicly available) and internal precedents
- **New Zealand**: The PCO publishes the *Legislation Guidelines* and the *Plain Language in Legislation* guide
- **Canada**: The Department of Justice publishes *Legistics* (a bilingual guide to drafting federal legislation)

## Legislative Quality Checklist

A quality legislative drafting checklist (adapted from the OECD checklist framework in [`oecd-checklist-law-drafting-regulatory-management.pdf`](oecd-checklist-law-drafting-regulatory-management.pdf)):

**Scope and coverage:**
- [ ] Does the legislation clearly identify the persons and conduct it regulates?
- [ ] Are there gaps (situations intended to be covered but accidentally omitted)?
- [ ] Are there unintended inclusions (situations that fall within the text but were not intended to be regulated)?

**Internal consistency:**
- [ ] Are defined terms used consistently throughout?
- [ ] Do the provisions interact without creating conflicts or redundancies?
- [ ] Are cross-references accurate?

**Constitutional validity:**
- [ ] Is the legislation within the legislative competence of the enacting legislature?
- [ ] Does it comply with constitutional rights and limitations?

**Clarity and accessibility:**
- [ ] Can the intended audience understand the legislation?
- [ ] Is the structure logical (general before specific; main rule before exceptions)?
- [ ] Are sentences within acceptable length limits?
- [ ] Has the plain language test been applied?

**Practical workability:**
- [ ] Can the legislation be enforced?
- [ ] Are the timeframes and thresholds workable in practice?
- [ ] Have the administrative requirements been considered?
