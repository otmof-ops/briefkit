## What You Will Learn

After reading this section, you will be able to:

- Apply plain language principles to all forms of legal writing without sacrificing precision
- Draft internal legal memoranda using IRAC and CREAC methods

- Draft affidavits and statutory declarations that satisfy evidentiary requirements
- Prepare pleadings (statements of claim, defenses, counterclaims) that comply with material-facts pleading rules

## Overview

Legal writing is the medium through which legal analysis is communicated. Every skill in legal practice --- research, advocacy, negotiation, compliance --- depends on the ability to express legal propositions clearly, accurately, and persuasively in written form.

This section covers seven categories of legal writing, from foundational principles through specific document types. The documents progress from general to specific: Document 01 establishes the principles that apply to all legal writing; Documents 02 through 06 address specific document types encountered in practice; Document 07 addresses the specialized field of legislative drafting.

The materials are jurisdiction-comparative, drawing on the writing traditions and court rules of Australia, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and New Zealand. European Union materials are addressed where relevant, particularly in the context of legislative drafting.

## Quality Standards

All written output produced using the methods described in this section should meet the following standards:

1. **Clarity**: The document can be understood by its intended audience on first reading
2. **Accuracy**: All factual statements are correct; all legal propositions are supported by cited authority
3. **Completeness**: The document addresses all issues within its stated scope
4. **Compliance**: The document complies with applicable court rules, practice directions, and professional obligations
5. **Currency**: All cited authorities are current and have been verified through citator checks

## Common Writing Pitfalls

Practitioners and students frequently encounter the following pitfalls:

1. **Over-qualification**: Hedging every statement to the point of uselessness. State conclusions clearly and qualify only where the uncertainty is genuine.
2. **Passive voice overuse**: Passive voice obscures the actor. Use active voice as the default; reserve passive for the specific situations identified in Document 01.
3. **Excessive length**: Longer is not better. Courts and clients value conciseness. Say what needs to be said and stop.
4. **Citation errors**: Incorrect citations undermine credibility immediately. Verify every citation before filing or sending.
5. **Ignoring the audience**: Writing that is too technical for clients or too informal for courts fails its purpose. Always write for the specific audience.
6. **Arguing in affidavits**: Affidavits contain facts, not arguments. Legal arguments belong in submissions.
7. **Pleading evidence instead of facts**: Pleadings state what happened, not the evidence that proves it. The evidence comes later.
