# Formative Assessment: Quick Reference

## Definition

Formative assessment is the use of assessment information to adjust teaching and learning during the instructional process. Unlike summative assessment, which evaluates what students have learned after instruction is complete, formative assessment monitors learning in progress and uses that information to guide instructional next steps. The purpose is not to grade or certify but to improve --- to identify where learners are, where they need to go, and how to get there.

The distinction is functional, not structural: the same assessment task (a quiz, a written response, a class discussion) can serve either a formative or summative purpose depending on how the information is used. When assessment data informs ongoing instructional decisions, it is formative. When it certifies achievement after instruction is complete, it is summative.

## Five Key Strategies (Black and Wiliam)

Paul Black and Dylan Wiliam's research program, synthesized in their foundational 1998 review "Inside the Black Box" and elaborated in subsequent publications, identified five key strategies that define formative assessment practice:

**Strategy 1: Clarifying learning intentions and success criteria.** Students learn more effectively when they understand what they are expected to learn and what successful performance looks like. Share learning objectives in student-friendly language at the beginning of each lesson. Provide exemplars of strong, adequate, and weak work with explicit commentary on what distinguishes each level. Co-construct success criteria with students to promote ownership and understanding.

**Strategy 2: Engineering effective classroom discussions and tasks that elicit evidence of student understanding.** Every classroom interaction is a potential source of formative data. Design questions and tasks that reveal the quality of student understanding --- not just whether students know the right answer but how they think about the problem. Use open-ended questions that require explanation rather than recall. Allow sufficient wait time (3-5 seconds minimum) for thoughtful responses. Use techniques like cold calling (randomly selecting students rather than relying on volunteers) to sample understanding across the class.

**Strategy 3: Providing feedback that moves learners forward.** Feedback is the engine of formative assessment. Effective feedback addresses the gap between current performance and the desired standard and provides specific guidance for closing that gap. The characteristics of effective feedback are discussed in detail in the section below.

**Strategy 4: Activating students as resources for one another.** Peer assessment and peer feedback develop both the assessor's and the assessee's understanding. When students evaluate peers' work against shared criteria, they internalize quality standards and develop metacognitive skills. Structure peer feedback activities with clear rubrics, sentence starters, and modeling of constructive feedback language.

**Strategy 5: Activating students as owners of their own learning.** Self-assessment and self-regulation are the ultimate goals of formative assessment. Students who can accurately assess their own understanding and strategically direct their own learning are less dependent on external feedback. Teach self-assessment explicitly through calibration activities (students assess their own work, then compare their assessment to the instructor's) and reflection prompts (what did you learn? what is still confusing? what will you do next?).

## Effective Feedback Characteristics

Research on feedback (Hattie and Timperley, 2007; Shute, 2008; Winne and Butler, 1994) identifies four characteristics of effective feedback:

**Task-focused.** Effective feedback addresses the quality of the work, not the qualities of the person. "Your thesis statement needs a clearer claim" is task-focused feedback. "You are a good writer" or "You need to try harder" is person-focused feedback that provides no actionable guidance. Research consistently shows that task-focused feedback produces better learning outcomes than person-focused feedback, which can undermine motivation (particularly for students who receive negative person-focused feedback) or produce complacency (for students who receive positive person-focused feedback without guidance for improvement).

**Timely.** Feedback is most effective when delivered while the learning task is still active and the learner can act on it. Delayed feedback (returning assignments weeks after submission) loses its formative value because the learner has moved on to new content and the connection between performance and feedback is weakened. For simple tasks and error correction, immediate feedback is optimal. For complex tasks requiring deep processing, a short delay (hours to a day) may be beneficial, allowing the learner to process the task before receiving external input.

**Actionable.** Effective feedback provides specific guidance for improvement --- not just what is wrong but what to do about it. "Unclear" is not actionable. "Your second paragraph introduces three ideas; focus on one and develop it with a specific example" is actionable. Feedback should answer three questions for the learner: where am I going? (the goal), where am I now? (current performance relative to the goal), and how do I get there? (specific next steps).

**Forward-looking.** Effective feedback orients toward future performance rather than merely evaluating past performance. It identifies specific strategies, skills, or knowledge that the learner should develop and provides a path for development. "In your next essay, try stating your counterargument before your rebuttal to strengthen your argumentative structure" is forward-looking feedback that the learner can apply immediately.

## Classroom Techniques

The following table presents commonly used formative assessment techniques, organized by implementation complexity:

| Technique | Description | Time Required | Evidence Gathered | Implementation Tips |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Think-pair-share** | Students think individually, discuss with a partner, then share with the class | 3-5 minutes | Conceptual understanding, reasoning processes | Pose a question that requires explanation, not just recall; circulate during pair discussions to listen for common patterns |
| **Exit tickets** | Students write a brief response to a prompt at the end of class | 2-3 minutes | Lesson comprehension, remaining questions | Use a single focused question; sort responses into "got it," "almost," and "not yet" to plan the next lesson |
| **Mini whiteboards** | Students write answers on individual whiteboards and hold them up simultaneously | 1-2 minutes per question | Whole-class snapshot of understanding | Use for checking procedural skills, vocabulary, or short-answer conceptual questions; scan for patterns rather than individual responses |
| **Peer review** | Students evaluate each other's work using a rubric or criteria checklist | 15-20 minutes | Quality of understanding (assessor); specific improvement guidance (assessee) | Model the peer review process explicitly; provide structured feedback forms; debrief common observations with the whole class |
| **One-minute paper** | Students write for one minute on what they learned and what remains unclear | 1-2 minutes | Self-assessed comprehension, misconception identification | Review responses before the next class; address common confusions in the opening minutes |
| **Concept maps** | Students create visual representations of concept relationships | 10-15 minutes | Knowledge structure, connections between ideas | Provide the key terms and ask students to arrange and connect them; compare student maps to expert maps |
| **Traffic light self-assessment** | Students indicate their confidence (green/yellow/red) on each learning objective | 1 minute | Self-assessed confidence, identifies students needing support | Use at the end of a topic; follow up with targeted support for yellow and red students |
| **Muddiest point** | Students identify the concept they found most confusing | 1-2 minutes | Specific points of confusion | Use when covering complex material; cluster responses to identify systematic misunderstandings |
| **Diagnostic multiple choice** | Multiple choice questions where wrong answers correspond to known misconceptions | 2-3 minutes per question | Specific misconceptions held by students | Design distractors based on research-documented misconceptions; discuss why each wrong answer is tempting |
| **Classroom polling** | Anonymous electronic polling on conceptual questions | 2-3 minutes per question | Distribution of understanding across the class | Use with peer instruction: poll, discuss, re-poll to see if discussion shifts understanding |

## Effect Sizes

Formative assessment is among the most effective educational interventions documented in the research literature. Black and Wiliam's (1998) review of over 250 studies reported effect sizes of 0.40-0.70 on student achievement --- equivalent to moving an average student from the 50th to the 65th-85th percentile.

For context, the following table compares formative assessment effect sizes to other common educational interventions:

| Intervention | Typical Effect Size (d) | Equivalent Percentile Gain |
|---|---|---|
| Formative assessment (well-implemented) | 0.40-0.70 | 15-26 percentile points |
| Feedback (task-focused, specific) | 0.50-0.73 | 19-27 percentile points |
| Direct instruction | 0.59 | 22 percentile points |
| Cooperative learning | 0.41 | 16 percentile points |
| Homework (secondary school) | 0.36 | 14 percentile points |
| Reducing class size (by 8 students) | 0.21 | 8 percentile points |
| Retention (repeating a grade) | -0.13 | -5 percentile points |

These effect sizes make formative assessment one of the highest-leverage instructional practices available. Importantly, the effects are largest for low-achieving students, making formative assessment an equity intervention as well as a learning intervention. The caveat is that effect sizes depend on implementation quality: formative assessment as a philosophy integrated into daily practice produces large effects; formative assessment as an occasional technique produces modest effects.

## Implementation Guidance

Effective formative assessment is not a technique to be added onto existing practice but a philosophy that transforms how teaching and learning interact. Begin by selecting one or two strategies from the table above and practicing them consistently. Develop routines so that formative assessment becomes automatic rather than effortful. Use the data gathered to make visible instructional adjustments --- students who see that their feedback influences what happens next are more likely to engage authentically with formative assessment processes.
