# Summative Assessment: Quick Reference

## Definition

Summative assessment evaluates student learning at the conclusion of an instructional period --- a unit, course, semester, or program --- for the purposes of certification, accountability, and signaling. Where formative assessment asks "How is the learning going and what should we adjust?", summative assessment asks "What has been learned and to what standard?" Summative assessments generate the grades, scores, credentials, and certifications that students, institutions, employers, and policymakers use to make consequential decisions.

The stakes of summative assessment demand technical rigor: the scores must be valid (measuring what they claim to measure), reliable (producing consistent results across occasions, raters, and items), and fair (not systematically advantaging or disadvantaging particular groups of students). Poorly designed summative assessments can misrepresent student learning, distort instructional priorities, and produce inequitable outcomes.

## Types of Summative Assessment

### Standardized Tests

Standardized tests administer the same items under the same conditions to all examinees, enabling score comparisons across individuals and groups. They are the primary tool for large-scale accountability and admissions decisions. Standardized tests excel at measuring declarative knowledge and well-defined procedural skills efficiently and reliably. Their limitations include restricted construct coverage (difficulty assessing complex reasoning, creativity, and collaborative skills), potential cultural and linguistic bias, and the risk that high-stakes testing narrows curriculum to tested content.

### Portfolios

Portfolios are curated collections of student work that demonstrate achievement, growth, and reflection over time. They can include written work, project artifacts, multimedia productions, self-reflections, and evidence of revision and improvement. Portfolios excel at capturing complex, authentic learning outcomes that standardized tests cannot assess. Their limitations include scoring reliability challenges (different raters may evaluate the same portfolio differently), the time required for both compilation and evaluation, and the difficulty of standardizing expectations across courses and institutions.

### Capstone Projects

Capstone projects are culminating assessments that require students to integrate and apply knowledge and skills from across a program of study to a substantial, authentic task. Common formats include research theses, design projects, community engagement projects, and professional simulations. Capstone projects provide the most authentic evidence of a student's readiness for professional practice or advanced study. Their limitations include resource intensity (faculty supervision time, materials, and evaluation effort) and the difficulty of ensuring comparable rigor across different project topics and supervisors.

## Rubric Design Principles

Well-designed rubrics improve the validity, reliability, and transparency of summative assessment. Effective rubrics share the following characteristics:

**Aligned with learning outcomes.** Each rubric criterion should map directly to one or more course or program learning outcomes. Criteria that do not connect to stated outcomes introduce construct-irrelevant variance --- scoring factors unrelated to the learning being assessed.

**Observable and specific descriptors.** Level descriptions should use concrete, observable language rather than vague qualifiers. "Identifies three relevant factors and explains the causal relationship between them" is observable. "Shows good understanding" is not. Specific descriptors reduce rater subjectivity and help students understand expectations.

**Meaningful level distinctions.** Each performance level should describe qualitatively different performance, not merely quantitative gradations. The difference between "proficient" and "developing" should reflect a genuine difference in understanding or skill, not just a difference in the number of errors.

**Appropriate number of levels.** Three to five levels provide sufficient discrimination for most purposes. Fewer than three levels provide too little differentiation; more than five levels create reliability problems because raters cannot consistently distinguish among many closely spaced levels.

**Analytic structure for formative use; holistic option for summative efficiency.** Analytic rubrics (separate scores for each criterion) provide more detailed feedback and are better suited for instruction. Holistic rubrics (a single overall score) are faster to apply and may be sufficient for purely summative purposes.

## Grading Scales

The following table presents common grading scale configurations used in higher education:

| Scale Type | Levels | Characteristics | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Percentage** | 0-100 | Fine-grained; intuitive for students; precision may exceed measurement accuracy | Assignment-level grading |
| **Letter grade (A-F)** | 5-13 (with +/-) | Familiar; maps to GPA; arbitrary cut points between grades | Course-level grading (North America) |
| **Criterion-referenced (HD/D/C/P/F)** | 5 | Descriptive; each grade defined by performance criteria | Course-level grading (Australia, UK) |
| **Pass/Fail** | 2 | Simplest; reduces grade anxiety; loses differentiation information | Clinical placements, electives, thesis |
| **Standards-based (1-4)** | 4 | Each level defined by mastery of specific standards; criterion-referenced | K-12 standards-based reporting |
| **Competency-based (Not Yet/Competent/Exemplary)** | 3 | Focused on demonstrated competence; allows reassessment | Vocational education, professional certification |

When selecting a grading scale, consider the assessment purpose (certification vs. feedback), the precision warranted by the measurement instrument, institutional policy requirements, and the communication needs of the intended audience (students, employers, graduate programs).

## Alignment with Learning Outcomes

Summative assessments must be aligned with stated learning outcomes at the item, task, and instrument level. Misalignment --- testing content that was not taught, testing at a cognitive level different from the learning outcome, or testing skills different from those practiced --- undermines validity and produces misleading scores.

Alignment is typically evaluated using a content-evidence matrix that maps each assessment item or task to the learning outcome(s) it addresses and the cognitive level (using Bloom's revised taxonomy or a similar framework) at which it operates. The matrix reveals gaps (outcomes not assessed), redundancies (outcomes over-assessed), and mismatches (assessment cognitive level does not match outcome cognitive level).

Evidence-centered design (ECD), developed by Mislevy, Steinberg, and Almond, provides a systematic framework for assessment design that builds alignment into the design process rather than checking it after the fact. ECD structures assessment around three linked models:

The student model defines the knowledge, skills, and abilities to be assessed. The evidence model defines what observable performances constitute evidence of those knowledge, skills, and abilities. The task model defines what assessment tasks will elicit those performances. Misalignment occurs when these three models are not coherently linked --- when the tasks do not elicit the evidence, or the evidence does not warrant inferences about the targeted knowledge, skills, and abilities.

## Standards-Based vs. Norm-Referenced Assessment

The following comparison table clarifies two fundamentally different approaches to score interpretation:

| Dimension | Standards-Based (Criterion-Referenced) | Norm-Referenced |
|---|---|---|
| **Score meaning** | Performance relative to defined standards | Performance relative to other examinees |
| **Interpretation** | "This student has mastered 85% of the required competencies" | "This student scored higher than 75% of test-takers" |
| **Purpose** | Certification, mastery decisions, standards compliance | Selection, ranking, competitive placement |
| **Score distribution** | Any distribution is valid; theoretically all students could achieve mastery | Distribution is informative; designed to maximize spread |
| **Item selection** | Items sample the content domain representatively | Items selected for maximum discrimination between examinees |
| **Cut scores** | Set based on performance standards (judgmental or empirical) | Set based on desired selection ratio or percentile |
| **Instructional sensitivity** | High --- scores should improve with effective instruction | Variable --- some norm-referenced tests resist instructional effects by design |
| **Example** | Professional licensing exams, competency checklists, standards-based report cards | University entrance exams, class ranking, IQ tests |
| **Strength** | Transparent standards; all students can potentially succeed | Efficient selection; reliable differentiation among examinees |
| **Limitation** | Standard-setting is judgmental and can be inconsistent | Does not indicate what students know; zero-sum competition |

The choice between standards-based and norm-referenced assessment is determined by purpose. When the question is "Has this student achieved the required standard?" (certification, licensing, mastery determination), criterion-referenced assessment is appropriate. When the question is "How does this student compare to others?" (selection, ranking, placement), norm-referenced assessment is appropriate.

## Validity and Reliability

### Validity

Validity is not a property of the test itself but of the interpretation and use of test scores. Messick's (1989) unified validity framework identifies five sources of validity evidence:

Content evidence: the degree to which test content represents the domain being assessed. Response process evidence: the degree to which examinees engage in the cognitive processes the test intends to measure. Internal structure evidence: the degree to which the statistical relationships among test items match the theoretical structure of the construct. Relations to other variables: the degree to which test scores correlate appropriately with external criteria (convergent and discriminant validity). Consequences evidence: the degree to which the social consequences of testing support the intended interpretation and use.

### Reliability

Reliability refers to the consistency of test scores across occasions, raters, items, and conditions. Generalizability theory decomposes score variance into multiple sources --- persons, items, raters, occasions, and their interactions --- enabling targeted identification and reduction of specific error sources.

Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity: a test can produce highly consistent scores that consistently measure the wrong thing. The practical implication is that reliability should be maximized within the constraint of validity --- that is, without sacrificing construct representation for the sake of scoring consistency.

## Implementation Guidance

Design summative assessments backward from learning outcomes: identify the evidence needed to warrant achievement claims, design tasks that elicit that evidence, and develop scoring criteria that capture the quality dimensions of the evidence. Use the content-evidence matrix to verify alignment. Pilot assessments with a small group and revise based on item analysis (difficulty, discrimination, distractor effectiveness for selected-response items; inter-rater reliability for constructed-response items). Provide students with assessment criteria and exemplars in advance --- transparency about expectations is not "giving away the answers" but an equity practice that ensures all students understand what is valued.
