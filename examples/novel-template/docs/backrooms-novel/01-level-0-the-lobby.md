# Part 1 — Level 0: The Lobby

## Chapter 1: The First Step

She did not fall. That was the first thing she understood when the carpet came up to meet her feet and stayed there.

She had been in a department store. She remembered that clearly: the particular quality of retail fluorescence, the smell of treated air and synthetic fabrics, the sound of a distant transaction terminal processing a return. She had been walking quickly — too quickly, probably, her eyes on her phone, navigating to the exit. And then the floor had gone slightly wrong underfoot, the way a staircase does when you misjudge the last step in the dark, and she had stumbled, and caught herself, and looked up.

The department store was gone.

The corridor stretched in both directions for as far as she could see, which was not very far because the fluorescent tubes in the ceiling emitted a light that was technically sufficient and practically oppressive, a light that illuminated surfaces without making distances comprehensible. The carpet beneath her feet was the color of old mustard, patterned in a repeating geometric that her eyes kept trying to resolve into a recognizable shape and failing. The walls were wallpaper over plasterboard, a creamy beige that had yellowed toward the color of old teeth, hung with nothing. No pictures. No signs. No doors, at first glance, and then too many doors, and then she was not sure the doors had always been there.

She stood still for a long time.

## Chapter 2: The Sound

The hum was the second thing she noticed, after the impossibility of the place had absorbed most of her immediate cognitive capacity. It was not loud. It was the frequency of fluorescent lighting, that particular 60-hertz drone that ordinarily sits below conscious attention and only surfaces when a building has been empty long enough that the hum is the loudest thing in it. Here it was always the loudest thing. She could not locate its source. Every tube, every section of corridor, every room she pushed a door open and peered into: the hum was the same volume, the same pitch, sourceless and omnipresent, as though the Backrooms themselves were vibrating at a frequency just above the threshold of structural resonance.

She would learn later, from accounts she found scratched into the plasterboard in places where other wanderers had stopped to mark their passage, that the hum never stopped. It was the single most consistent feature of Level 0. Entities came and went. Geometry shifted. The wallpaper pattern changed its dominant color across different regions she would later understand as zones. But the hum remained.

She told herself it was a comfort. After three days she believed it.

## Chapter 3: Orientation

The rules of Level 0, as she worked them out over the first week:

Food and water were problems. She found a vending machine on the second day, the face of it cracked at one corner, the mechanism still operational. It accepted nothing but dispensed at random: crackers in the paper sleeves common to institutional breakrooms, small bottles of water, and occasionally a can of a beverage that smelled faintly of almonds and tasted of nothing she could precisely identify. She drank it anyway. She felt better than she had a right to.

Direction was a problem. Level 0's geometry did not obey Euclidean expectations. She mapped as she moved, marking walls with a pen she'd had in her jacket pocket, but the marks were not always where she expected them when she retraced a route. Whether the architecture was genuinely changing or whether her own spatial perception was degrading, she could not determine. She stopped trusting her mapped routes and started trusting her instincts instead, which was terrifying. Her instincts, improbably, proved more reliable.

She was not alone. That was both the most frightening discovery and, ultimately, the one that would save her. The sound she heard on the fourth night — a soft, unhurried something moving on the carpet two corridors over, a sound that was not quite footsteps because footsteps imply a foot, and whatever was making that sound was making contact with the carpet in a distributed, irregular way that suggested nothing human — was the first entity. She did not see it. She pressed herself into a corner behind a door and held her breath and it passed, and the hum absorbed what might have been the sound of its breathing, and it was gone.

She moved differently after that.
