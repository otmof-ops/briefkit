# Part 2 — Level 1: Pipe Dreams

## Chapter 4: The Crossing

She found the transition to Level 1 by accident, which was the only way anyone found it.

She had been following a long corridor that had been running northeast by her estimate for most of a day — she had long since lost reliable track of time, the fluorescent light being seasonal-affective-disorder-constant, never dimming, never brightening, giving no signal — when she noticed that the carpet had changed. Not the pattern. The texture. It was the same unhappy mustard, the same insistent geometry, but it was no longer soft underfoot. It had compressed into something almost rigid, and when she pressed her palm to it she could feel through the backing the faint vibration of pipes.

The walls here were concrete, not plasterboard. The ceiling was lower. The hum was still present but it had a different harmonic now, layered over something that might have been the movement of water or the movement of something else through the pipes whose presence she felt but could not see.

The lights here were not fluorescent tubes. They were bare incandescent bulbs, strung at intervals of approximately four meters, the wire looping in long catenary curves between brackets sunk into the concrete. The light was warmer and less reliable. Some bulbs had failed. Some were in the process of failing, cycling through a slow orange fade that she tried not to watch because watching it made the darkness that followed feel permanent.

This was Level 1.

## Chapter 5: Water and Warmth

The pipes were real. She found where they emerged from the concrete: runs of industrial galvanized steel, some as wide as her forearm, some as narrow as her thumb, running in parallel along the ceiling and then branching down to junction boxes that connected to nothing visible. She pressed her palm against one and felt heat. Not scalding. Not even uncomfortable. The warmth of a domestic radiator, steady and mechanical, carrying the memory of a boiler somewhere in the infrastructure of this place.

She sat with her back against a warm pipe for a long time and let herself feel something other than cold.

Level 1 had more water than Level 0. She found it standing in low depressions in the concrete floor, pooled and still, with the faint almond odor she had come to associate with the vending machine cans. She drank from it despite every piece of conventional wisdom she possessed about drinking standing water in enclosed spaces. She was thirsty enough. She was still standing afterward, which was its own kind of evidence.

The entities of Level 1 were different from Level 0's corridor-walker. They were smaller and faster and they moved in groups, and they communicated in a register just above the range of the pipes' ambient noise in a way that made them hard to locate by sound alone. She saw one clearly once: a thing about the size of a large cat but with the body plan of neither cat nor any other animal she recognized, pale and smooth and moving with the peculiar mechanical fluency of something that had never learned to hesitate. It saw her. It stopped. It made the sound. Then it moved on, and the group moved with it.

She did not understand why it had left her alone. She was grateful that she could not understand it.

## Chapter 6: The Other Wanderers

The note was written on the back of a logistics label, the kind used for warehouse inventory, stuck to a junction box with a strip of electrical tape. The handwriting was small and careful: "Level 2 accessible via the maintenance corridor on the east wall of the large room. Room has a broken bulb at the entrance. Do not stop moving through the room. There is something in the ceiling."

She read it three times.

She had been alone for eleven days by her estimate. The note was the first evidence that another person had passed through this specific point. She did not know when it had been written. The ink was not faded but in Level 1 light was not reliable enough to read degradation. The label's adhesive had dried and re-set in the way adhesives do over weeks rather than days.

She wrote beneath the note, in the margin of the label: "Arrived from Level 0 northeast corridor. Found water. Entities present but not aggressive when moving. Still here. Still going."

She dated it as best she could. She did not know if anyone would ever read it. She wrote it anyway because writing it made her real in a way that the Backrooms, with their determination to be limitless and undifferentiated, were constantly working to undo.

She found the large room. She found the broken bulb. She moved through without stopping.
