# Preface

The Backrooms began as a single image: a photograph of an empty office corridor, yellow-lit, carpeted in a pattern that seemed to go on forever. No doors. No windows. No people. The photograph circulated online with a caption that said something like "if you're not careful and you no-clip out of reality in the wrong areas, you'll end up in the Backrooms." That caption — absurd, specific, quietly dreadful — launched a thousand stories.

This novel is one of them. It does not claim to be the definitive account. The Backrooms resist definition. Every expedition that has attempted to map the levels has returned with a different count, different geometries, different estimates of scale. The architecture changes. The rules shift. The only constant is the sound: the low, arrhythmic hum of fluorescent lights running on power from no discernible source, in a building that has no discernible end.

What follows is the account of one person's passage through three levels of the Backrooms and, eventually, out the other side. The account has been reconstructed from written notes, audio recordings, and the survivor's own testimony. Where those sources conflict, the account follows the testimony. Where testimony failed, the account follows the silence.

You have been warned. Keep moving. Do not look directly at anything that moves in your peripheral vision. If you hear something that sounds like your name, do not respond. The Backrooms remember nothing, but they learn.
