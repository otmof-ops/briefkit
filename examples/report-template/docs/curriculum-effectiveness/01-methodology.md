# 1. Research Methodology

## 1.1 Study Overview

This assessment was commissioned by the National Education Board to evaluate the
effectiveness of structured curriculum design methodologies relative to traditional
approaches. The study was conducted between September 2024 and June 2025 across
34 pilot schools representing a cross-section of urban, suburban, and rural
educational environments. The primary objective was to generate an evidence base
sufficient to inform national curriculum policy decisions for the 2027-2030
planning cycle.

The study addressed three principal research questions:

1. Does backward design, as defined by the Understanding by Design (UbD) framework
   (Wiggins and McTighe, 2005), produce measurably superior student outcomes when
   compared with traditional content-first curriculum design?
2. What is the impact of integrating structured active learning strategies into
   existing curricula, and what proportion of instructional time must be allocated
   to active learning to achieve statistically significant improvements?
3. Do competency-based curriculum models produce more consistent graduate skill
   profiles than traditional time-based progression models?

## 1.2 Study Design

The assessment employed a quasi-experimental controlled comparison design. The
34 pilot schools were organized into three cohorts:

- **Cohort A (12 schools):** Implemented the backward design framework across all
  assessed subject areas. Teachers received 40 hours of pre-implementation training
  and ongoing instructional coaching throughout the academic year.
- **Cohort B (11 schools):** Integrated structured active learning strategies into
  existing curricula without modifying the underlying curriculum design. A minimum
  threshold of 15 percent of instructional time devoted to active learning was
  established, with a target range of 20-30 percent.
- **Cohort C (11 schools):** Served as the control group, continuing with existing
  curriculum designs and instructional practices. Control schools received no
  additional training or resources during the study period.

Schools were assigned to cohorts using stratified random assignment to ensure
balance across geographic setting (urban, suburban, rural), prior student
achievement levels, and school size. Matching was verified using baseline
assessment data collected in September 2024; no statistically significant
differences were found between cohorts on any baseline measure (all p > 0.15).

The assessment focused on three subject areas: science (grades 6-8), mathematics
(grades 6-8), and English language arts (grades 9-10). These subjects were
selected because they represent a range of disciplinary structures and because
validated assessment instruments were available for each.

## 1.3 Data Collection Methods

Data were collected through four primary instruments, each selected to capture
a distinct dimension of curriculum effectiveness.

### 1.3.1 Standardized Pre- and Post-Instruction Assessments

All students in assessed subject areas completed standardized assessments at the
beginning (September 2024) and end (May 2025) of the academic year. Assessments
were drawn from the National Assessment Item Bank and were aligned to the
National Curriculum Standards. Each assessment included 60 items: 30 factual
recall items, 20 application items, and 10 transfer items requiring students to
apply knowledge to novel contexts.

Pre-instruction assessments established baselines for each student. Post-instruction
assessments measured learning gains. The use of pre-post gains rather than absolute
post-test scores controlled for differences in entering knowledge across schools
and cohorts.

A total of 8,216 students completed both pre- and post-instruction assessments.
Attrition was 7.4 percent, distributed evenly across cohorts.

### 1.3.2 Structured Classroom Observations

Trained observers conducted 408 structured classroom observations across the 34
pilot schools (12 observations per school on average). Observations were scheduled
at random intervals to capture representative instructional practice. Each
observation lasted 45 minutes and was coded using the Classroom Observation
Protocol for Undergraduate STEM (COPUS), adapted for the middle and secondary
school context.

Observers recorded the proportion of class time devoted to lecturing, active
learning, administrative tasks, and assessment. Inter-rater reliability was
established through calibration sessions; Cohen's kappa exceeded 0.82 for all
observation categories.

### 1.3.3 Student Experience Surveys

Students in grades 8 and 10 completed end-of-year surveys measuring perceived
engagement, self-reported metacognitive awareness, and satisfaction with
instructional methods. The survey instrument included 35 Likert-scale items and
three open-response prompts. Survey response rate was 89.2 percent (n = 4,891
of 5,484 eligible students).

### 1.3.4 Teacher Implementation Logs

Teachers in Cohorts A and B maintained weekly implementation logs documenting the
specific curriculum design or active learning strategies employed, time allocated,
challenges encountered, and perceived student response. Logs were reviewed monthly
by the research team to monitor implementation fidelity.

## 1.5 Analysis Framework

The analysis proceeded in three stages, each corresponding to a research question.

**Stage 1: Backward Design Effectiveness.** Learning gains for Cohort A were
compared with Cohort C using analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) with pre-test scores
as the covariate. Effect sizes were computed using Cohen's d with pooled standard
deviations. Subgroup analyses examined effects by subject area, grade level,
and student demographic characteristics.

**Stage 2: Active Learning Impact.** Learning gains and failure rates for Cohort B
were compared with Cohort C using the same ANCOVA framework. Additionally, a
dose-response analysis examined the relationship between the proportion of
instructional time devoted to active learning (as measured by classroom
observations) and student outcomes. The dose-response model was fitted using
piecewise linear regression to capture the nonlinear relationship identified in
prior research (Freeman et al., 2014).

**Stage 3: Competency-Based Profiling.** For a subset of 1,240 students in Cohort A
schools that had adopted competency-based assessment alongside backward design,
skill profiles were generated using rubric-scored performance tasks. The
consistency of skill profiles was compared with matched students in Cohort C
using the coefficient of variation across competency domains.

All analyses controlled for school-level clustering using hierarchical linear
modeling with students nested within classrooms nested within schools. Statistical
significance was evaluated at alpha = 0.05 with Bonferroni correction for multiple
comparisons. Effect sizes were interpreted using the What Works Clearinghouse
standards: small (d = 0.05-0.20), medium (d = 0.20-0.50), and large (d > 0.50).

## 1.6 Limitations

Several limitations should be noted. First, the quasi-experimental design does not
fully eliminate selection bias, despite stratified assignment and baseline matching.
Second, teacher implementation fidelity varied across schools; not all Cohort B
teachers achieved the 15 percent active learning threshold consistently. Third,
the one-year study duration may not capture long-term effects of curriculum
redesign. Fourth, the assessment instruments, while aligned to national standards,
may not fully capture the deeper conceptual understanding that backward design
and active learning are theorized to promote. These limitations are addressed in
the interpretation of findings.
