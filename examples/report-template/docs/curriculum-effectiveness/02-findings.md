# 2. Findings

## 2.1 Overview

This section presents the results of the controlled comparison across the three
cohorts. Findings are organized by research question and supplemented with
subgroup analyses where differences were substantively meaningful. All reported
effect sizes use Cohen's d with pooled standard deviations; all p-values reflect
Bonferroni-corrected thresholds.

## 2.2 Backward Design Effectiveness

### 2.2.1 Primary Outcomes

Schools implementing the backward design framework (Cohort A) demonstrated
statistically significant improvements in student learning gains relative to
control schools (Cohort C). Across all three subject areas, the aggregate effect
size was d = 0.38 (95% CI: 0.29-0.47, p < 0.001). This effect falls within the
medium range under What Works Clearinghouse standards and is consistent with the
prior controlled study of 34 middle school science units reported by Wiggins and
McTighe (2005).

The improvement was observed across all item types, but was most pronounced on
transfer items requiring application of knowledge to novel contexts (d = 0.52),
followed by application items (d = 0.39) and factual recall items (d = 0.21).
This pattern supports the theoretical prediction that backward design promotes
deeper understanding rather than surface-level memorization, because the framework
explicitly aligns instruction with assessments that require transfer and application.

### 2.2.2 Results by Subject Area

| Subject Area          | Cohort A Effect Size (d) | 95% CI       | p-value  | Cohort A Mean Gain | Control Mean Gain |
|-----------------------|--------------------------|--------------|----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Science (Grades 6-8)  | 0.42                     | 0.30 - 0.54  | < 0.001  | 18.7 points        | 12.4 points        |
| Mathematics (Grades 6-8) | 0.35                  | 0.22 - 0.48  | < 0.001  | 16.2 points        | 11.1 points        |
| English Language Arts (Grades 9-10) | 0.36       | 0.21 - 0.51  | < 0.001  | 14.9 points        | 10.3 points        |
| **All subjects (aggregate)** | **0.38**           | **0.29 - 0.47** | **< 0.001** | **16.6 points** | **11.3 points** |

The largest effect was observed in science, which may reflect the discipline's
strong tradition of inquiry-based outcomes that align naturally with backward
design principles. Mathematics showed the smallest effect among the three, though
it remained statistically and practically significant.

### 2.2.3 Demographic Subgroup Analysis

Backward design benefits were observed across all demographic subgroups. Notably,
students from economically disadvantaged backgrounds showed a slightly larger
effect (d = 0.44) than their peers (d = 0.34), narrowing the achievement gap
by approximately 12 percent. Students receiving English language learner services
also showed above-average gains (d = 0.41), suggesting that the explicit
alignment of outcomes, assessments, and activities provides a scaffolding
structure that benefits students who may be disadvantaged by less structured
instructional approaches.

## 2.3 Active Learning Integration

### 2.3.1 Failure Rate Reduction

The most striking finding from Cohort B was the reduction in course failure rates.
Across all assessed subjects, the failure rate in Cohort B schools was 21.2 percent,
compared with 32.6 percent in Cohort C schools — a relative reduction of 35 percent.
When restricted to the pre-registered primary outcome (science, grades 6-8), the
failure rate reduction was 33 percent (from 30.1 percent to 20.2 percent),
replicating the finding reported in the Freeman et al. (2014) meta-analysis of
225 studies.

| Metric                          | Cohort B (Active Learning) | Cohort C (Control) | Difference       |
|---------------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------|------------------|
| Overall failure rate            | 21.2%                      | 32.6%              | -11.4 pp (-35%)  |
| Science failure rate            | 20.2%                      | 30.1%              | -9.9 pp (-33%)   |
| Mathematics failure rate        | 23.8%                      | 34.2%              | -10.4 pp (-30%)  |
| ELA failure rate                | 18.1%                      | 28.7%              | -10.6 pp (-37%)  |
| Mean exam score effect size (d) | 0.47                       | —                  | p < 0.001        |

The aggregate effect size on exam scores was d = 0.47 (95% CI: 0.37-0.57,
p < 0.001), approximately half a standard deviation — again consistent with
the Freeman et al. (2014) estimate. This represents a meaningful improvement:
a student at the 50th percentile under traditional instruction would be expected
to perform at approximately the 68th percentile under active learning conditions.

### 2.3.2 Dose-Response Relationship

Classroom observation data enabled analysis of the dose-response relationship
between active learning time and student outcomes. The piecewise linear regression
revealed three distinct zones:

| Active Learning Dosage      | Marginal Effect on Exam Scores | Cumulative Effect Size |
|-----------------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| 0-10% of class time         | Minimal (d < 0.10)            | 0.08                   |
| 10-15% of class time        | Moderate (threshold effect)    | 0.27                   |
| 15-30% of class time        | Strong linear gains            | 0.47                   |
| 30-50% of class time        | Continued gains, decelerating  | 0.56                   |
| Above 50% of class time     | Diminishing returns            | 0.58                   |

The most efficient investment occurs in the 15-30 percent range, where each
additional percentage point of active learning time yields approximately 0.013
standard deviations of improvement. Below 10 percent, active learning episodes
are too infrequent to establish the cognitive and metacognitive habits that drive
improvement. Above 50 percent, the marginal return per additional percentage
point drops to approximately 0.002 standard deviations.

This finding has direct policy implications: mandating a minimum threshold of
15 percent active learning time appears to be the most cost-effective intervention
point, capturing the majority of achievable gains without requiring wholesale
transformation of instructional practice.

### 2.3.3 Engagement and Metacognition

Student survey data corroborated the assessment findings. Students in Cohort B
reported significantly higher engagement (M = 3.82 vs. 3.21 on a 5-point scale,
p < 0.001) and metacognitive awareness (M = 3.67 vs. 3.09, p < 0.001). Open-response
analysis revealed recurring themes: 72 percent of Cohort B respondents referenced
activities that helped them "realize what I didn't understand" — consistent with
the metacognitive monitoring mechanism described in the active learning literature.

Collaborative active learning activities (peer discussion, group problem-solving)
were rated more favorably than individual active learning activities (solo
problem-solving, individual writing prompts) by a margin of 0.41 points on the
engagement scale (p < 0.01). This is consistent with social learning theory
predictions that peer interaction contributes independently to learning outcomes.

## 2.4 Competency-Based Assessment

### 2.4.1 Skill Profile Consistency

Among the 1,240 students in Cohort A schools that implemented competency-based
assessment alongside backward design, skill profiles showed substantially greater
consistency than matched students in control schools. The coefficient of variation
across six competency domains was 0.18 for competency-based students versus 0.31
for control students — a 42 percent reduction in skill-level variability.

| Competency Domain           | Cohort A CV | Control CV | Reduction |
|-----------------------------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| Factual Knowledge           | 0.14        | 0.22       | 36%       |
| Conceptual Understanding    | 0.17        | 0.29       | 41%       |
| Procedural Application      | 0.19        | 0.33       | 42%       |
| Analytical Reasoning        | 0.21        | 0.36       | 42%       |
| Communication               | 0.16        | 0.28       | 43%       |
| Collaborative Problem-Solving | 0.20      | 0.38       | 47%       |
| **Mean across domains**     | **0.18**    | **0.31**   | **42%**   |

This means that competency-based graduates present to subsequent educational
stages with more predictable and uniform skill sets. The largest consistency
improvement was observed in collaborative problem-solving (47 percent reduction
in CV), likely because competency-based programs require explicit demonstration
of this skill — something traditional assessment rarely measures.

### 2.4.2 Mastery Thresholds

In competency-based schools, 78.4 percent of students met or exceeded the
mastery threshold across all six domains, compared with 51.2 percent in control
schools. The gap was most pronounced in higher-order domains (analytical reasoning:
71.3 percent vs. 42.8 percent; collaborative problem-solving: 74.1 percent vs.
39.6 percent), suggesting that competency-based approaches are particularly
effective at ensuring development of complex skills that traditional curricula
often leave to incidental learning.

## 2.5 Summary of Key Findings

| Finding                                               | Magnitude              | Confidence |
|-------------------------------------------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Backward design improves standardized assessment scores | d = 0.38              | High       |
| Active learning reduces failure rates                  | 33% reduction          | High       |
| Active learning improves exam scores                   | d = 0.47              | High       |
| 15% active learning threshold captures majority of gains | Dose-response confirmed | Moderate |
| Competency-based programs reduce skill variability     | 42% CV reduction       | Moderate   |
| Backward design narrows achievement gap                | 12% gap reduction      | Moderate   |
| Effects are consistent across subjects and demographics | Subgroup analyses      | High       |

These findings converge on a clear conclusion: structured curriculum design
(backward design), active learning integration, and competency-based assessment
each independently improve student outcomes, and their effects are complementary.
Schools implementing multiple interventions simultaneously showed the largest
aggregate gains, though the study design does not permit precise estimation of
interaction effects.
